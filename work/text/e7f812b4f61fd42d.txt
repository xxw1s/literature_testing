Materials Science and Engineering A255 (1998) 107 – 116 Formation and coarsening of a nanodispersed microstructure in melt spun Al–Ni–Zr alloy Dheepa Srinivasan, K. Chattopadhyay * Department of Metallurgy, Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore 560 012, India Received 17 February 1998; received in revised form 8 June 1998 Abstract The paper reports a detailed investigation on the microstructure evolution during rapid solidification and subsequent heat treatment of an Al89Ni10Zr1 alloy and evaluates the role of Zr in the phase selection and the stability of the microstructure. It is shown that the as solidified alloy consists of grains of aluminium solid solution containing fine nickel rich clusters having an Al3Ni crystal structure. Low temperature heat treatments lead to further development of these Al3Ni particles yielding a uniform nanodispersed microstructure. The dispersed phase does not have any orientation relationship with the matrix. The composite displays very high hardness values (  5 GPa). At higher temperatures, the super saturated solid solution precipitated a metastable Al3Zr phase, less than 5 nm in size, which was found to be very stable to coarsening even up to 425°C. The Al3Zr also precipitated inside the Al3Ni phase indicating the partition of Zr in both the phases. However, it coarsened at a higher rate inside this phase. The presence of Al3Zr was also found to be effective in resisting coarsening of the matrix grains (Al), due to the similarities in structure and lattice constants between Al and Al3Zr. To bring out the effect produced by the added Zr, a binary counterpart was studied. Zr was found to have an effect equivalent to that produced due to a higher quench. © 1998 Elsevier Science S.A. Introduction There have been numerous reports on the enhanced mechanical properties in aluminium based nanocrystalline alloys [1– 3]. However the high temperature properties often suffer due to rapid coarsening. The design of nanocrystalline alloys, therefore, demands an attention into the issue of stability. Alloying additions which promote dispersions of a second phase with a low interfacial energy between the matrix phase and the second phase may hold promise in this direction. In this context Zr has a special place in the nanocrystalline aluminium alloy development. Supersaturated dilute alloys of Al with Zr have been found to precipitate out a coherent metastable Al3Zr phase [4 – 6]. By virtue of the small lattice misfit between the precipitate and the Al * Corresponding author. In the ternary alloys containing transition metals, Zr promotes glass formation and inhibits growth of the crystalline phase [11,12]. The decrease in mass transport provides opportunities for reducing the scale of the microstructure. Thus a judicial combination of chemistry and processing conditions can yield nanocrystalline composite structures which should also be stable against coarsening. Following the above reasoning, we explore in this paper the microstructural development of a rapidly solidified Al89Ni10Zr1 alloy and its subsequent decomposition behaviour at low and elevated temperatures in order to assess the possibility of developing high strength nanostructured aluminium alloys. The present work is part of a program for studying glassy and nanostructured Al–Ni alloys. Experimental details A ternary Al89Ni10Zr1 alloy was prepared by induction melting high purity (\99.999%) elemental mixtures. The alloy was rapidly solidified (cooling rate of 106 K s − 1) by melt spinning on to a rotating Cu wheel with a wheel surface velocity of 45 m s − 1. The thermal stability of the alloy was evaluated by ageing at four different temperatures (423, 523, 623 and 723 K) for various times. An Al – 7 at.% Ni hypereutectic binary alloy was also studied for comparison.This was rapidly solidified at a much higher cooling rate of  107 K s − 1, using the gun quenching technique. The microstructure was examined by transmission electron microscopy (JEOL 2000 FX II operating at 200 kV). Thin foils for microscopy were prepared using a GATAN duo ion mill, using Ar ions, with a sputtering angle ranging from 10 – 15°. Vickers microhardness measurements were made at a load of 10 g using a Shimadzu HMV 2000 microhardness tester. The extremely small size, however, prevented the determination of the exact nature of this phase. 3.1.2. Solidification sequence The compositions of aluminium and nickel in the ternary alloy under investigation correspond to hypereutectic composition for the binary alloy. The addition of 1% Zr does not alter the phase field significantly and one expects, on solidification, an eutectic microstructure of a-Al and intermetallic Al3Ni along with the primary Al3Ni phase. The orientation relationship between the two eutectic phases, Al and Al3Ni in an eutectic grain obtained during normal solidification has been documented in several earlier studies [14,15]. However, the fine eutectic grains, when observed in the rapidly solidified ternary alloy do not reveal any such orientation relation, clearly establishing the absence of such a relation at high growth rate. The eutectic diagram ensures that at hypereutectic compositions, there exists no driving force for the nucleation of a-Al at low undercooling. Only when undercooling is sufficiently high for the melt to enter the coupled zone, a driving force for nucleation of a-Al develops [16]. Our results on rapid solidification of binary alloy suggest that under extreme condition of rapid solidification, limited regions exhibit equiaxed solid solution grains with fine Al3Ni dispersions. However, most of the time, an eutectic morphology is observed. Although the ternary composition is more hypereutectic and cooling rates obtainable in the melt spun alloys (105–106 K s − 1) are lower than that of gun quenching (106 to 107 K s − 1), the addition of 1% Zr clearly yields a similar effect, with larger areas exhibiting solid solution microstructure. This leads to the conclusion that the addition of Zr has promoted undercooling similar to that achievable under very high heat transfer conditions. The occurrence of eutectic by a coupled growth of Al3Ni and a-Al in our samples, is in general associated with primary Al3Ni particles surrounded by the eutectic colony. It is therefore, most likely that addition of Zr inhibits the nucleation of Al3Ni phase resulting in the undercooling of the melt. The actual microstructure selection under such a circumstance depends on the kinetics of nucleation of the two competing phases in the undercooled liquid. In the absence of Al3Ni, a highly metastable aluminium solid solution will result. A significant observation of the present investigation is the distinct feathery morphology of the grains of the aluminium solid solution in the as solidified state. It is clear that the fine randomly dispersed clusters observed in the interdendritic regions of the as quenched solid solution must be playing an important role in the development of these random Al3Ni particles during subsequent heat treatment. Since the fine Al3Ni phase formed directly from the interdendritic liquid, they do not show any orientation relation. Subsequent heterogenous nucleation of Al3Ni during solid state heat treatment on these pre-existing particles will yield a random orientation of the Al3Ni particles. During heat treatment, the Al3Ni rods in the eutectic break down due to a perturbation induced by the coarsening [18]. The fine Al3Ni dispersion obtained by this mechanism also yields a non-oriented dispersion with the matrix, a direct outcome of the lack of orientation relation in the eutectic grain. 4.3. The formation and stability of cubic Al3Zr Our results suggest a high stability for Zr as a solute up to a temperature of 423 K. The Zr partitions to both a-Al and Al3Ni phases and subsequently precipitates out of both of them as a metastable cubic Al3Zr phase. The formation of this metastable phase coherently in aluminium matrix is well known [3 – 10] and can be rationalised by the advantage that this phase will have with respect to nucleation, due to low interfacial energy brought about by the coherency. The coarsening behaviour of the precipitate varies markedly in the two matrix grains. This is an outcome of the difference in the interfacial energies at the Al3Zrmatrix interface, in the two matrices of a-Al and Al3Ni. There is a five fold increase in the sizes of both Al3Ni as well as the Al3Zr that precipitated out of Al3Ni grains (on ageing from 5 min up to 8 h at 723 K). In comparison, the Al3Zr particles present in the a-Al grains do not grow significantly during the ageing process (Fig. 6a, b).We also note that no equilibrium tetragonal Al3Zr phase was seen at any time, in this study. The precipitate coarsening roughly follows a t 3 law within the grains, and has a t 2 dependence along the boundaries. The distribution clearly reveals the extraordinary stability of the Al3Zr particles nucleated inside the grains with significant stability of particles with less than 20 nm size. In contrast the distribution of the grain boundary nucleated particles are broader with large size range which evolves with time towards the bigger size. The high hardness values of the Al–Ni –Zr alloy indicate the possibility of developing very high strength alloys. This high value of as spun sample is attributed to two factors. The fine eutectic microstructure having alternating phases of Al3Ni rods and a-Al contributes to the high strength by a Hall–Petch type of strengthening. The a-Al grains with the trapped solute also gives rise to a some sort of solid solution strengthening. The low temperature heat treatment yields a fine, but discrete dispersion of Al3Ni in an a-Al matrix with Zr still in solid solution to provide strengthening of the two phases. This reduces the strength from that observed for the as spun alloys only marginally. However, one expects an increase in ductility. The nanocomposites obtained by the above route can have significant potential and further studies in this direction are currently in progress. At higher temperatures of 623 and 723 K, coarsening of all the phases including the precipitate phase, takes place, thereby causing a decrease in the hardness values. 5. Conclusions A nanodispersed microstructure of Al3Ni particles in an a-Al matrix was obtained after low temperature annealing of the melt spun alloy. No orientation relationship exists between the Al3Ni precipitates and a-Al matrix as they precipitate on the fine interdendritic Ni rich segregates and inherit the random orientation that they solidified in from the liquid. Both, the as spun alloy as well as the one after low temperature heat treatment showed high hardness values (500 VHN), brought about by the nanoscaled microstructue and the trapped solute. The effect of Zr as a solute on the as solidified microstructure has been equivalent to that produced by a higher quenching rate. At high temperatures precipitation and coarsening lead to a lower hardness.
