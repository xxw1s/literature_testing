Scale Rules for Macrosegregation during Direct-Chill Casting of Aluminum Alloys DMITRY G. ESKIN, QIANG DU, and LAURENS KATGERMAN An analysis of published experimental and numerical results shows that there is a scaling relationship between the magnitude and direction of centerline segregation in direct-chill (DC) cast billets from aluminum alloys and the process parameters, i.e., billet diameter and casting speed. It seems that there is always a range of these process parameters where the centerline segregation is positive, and there is a threshold when the centerline segregation vanishes. Numerical simulations of macrosegregation during DC casting of a binary Al-Cu alloy were performed at diﬀerent ratios of casting speed and billet diameter. The macrosegregation model takes into account only two mechanisms of macrosegregation, i.e., thermosolutal convection and shrinkage-induced ﬂow. The results of these computer simulations ﬁt well to the dependence obtained using numerous reference data. The results are discussed in terms of the contribution of diﬀerent mechanisms of macrosegregation and the shape of the billet sump. INTRODUCTION MACROSEGREGATION is a widely spread, unrecoverable defect of large-scale billets and ingots produced by a direct-chill (DC) casting route. The fundamental cause of chemical inhomogeneity (macrosegregation) is in the relative movement of solute-rich liquid and solute-lean solid during solidiﬁcation. There are four well-adopted mechanisms of macrosegregation: thermosolutal convection and transport of solid grains in the slurry and upper mushy zone, and shrinkageinduced ﬂow and deformation of the solid network in the mushy zone. It is clear that the overall macrosegregation pattern is a result of the diﬀerent contributions of these mechanisms, as summarized in Table I.[1,2] When it comes to practice, it is established that the size of the casting and the casting speed are the main parameters that determine the extent of macrosegregation.[1,3] There is a view that the negative centerline segregation is the sole result of accumulation in this region of solute-lean ‘‘ﬂoating’’ crystals.[4] This standpoint is, however, in contrast with (a) experimental observations on negative centerline segregation in billets and ingots without ﬂoating crystals[5] and (b) numerical studies that allow one to obtain negative segregation by invoking only the shrinkage-induced ﬂow without the transport of the solid phase.[6,7] The empirical rule says that the casting speed should not exceed A/D in order to minimize the macrosegregation in a round billet while maintaining the required DMITRY G. In this ratio, A is an alloy-dependent constant ranging from 25,000 to 33,300 for aluminum alloys and D is the billet diameter in mm.[8] Hence for a 200-mm billet, the maximum allowable casting speed should be between 125 and 167 mm/min. Livanov et al.[3] studied macrosegregation in a wide range of billet diameters, from 50 to 470 mm, and of casting speeds from 30 to 350 mm/min. They showed that the extent and direction of macrosegregation depends on the average cooling rate in the solidiﬁcation range. There exists a certain cooling rate for each billet diameter, when the centerline macrosegregation is negligible. For example, this threshold cooling rate is about 2.3 K/s for the 300-mm billet diameter and almost 11 K/s for the 168-mm diameter. Evidently, the cooling rate cannot be a universal criterion for macrosegregation. Livanov et al. then made a remarkable conclusion that the similarity of thermal and strain ﬁelds should lead to a similarity in macrosegregation patterns. Hence, they suggested a similarity criterion:[3] K ¼ Vcast D=a ½1 where Vcast is the casting speed in mm/min, D is the billet diameter in millimeters, and a is the constant that is about 11,500 (for normal molds without hot tops) for zero-centerline segregation. If K is less than unity, the macrosegregation is positive in the center. If K is larger than unity, the macrosegregation is negative in the center. The transition from positive to negative macrosegregation for a 200-mm billet should occur at a casting speed of about 60 mm/min, which is far below the optimum (and commercially applicable) speed. II. Surface Macrosegregation COMPUTATIONAL PROCEDURES The computer simulations performed in this work had two aims: (1) to check if the model suggested in our previous work[7] reproduces the general trend in macrosegregation scaling patterns and (2) to extract some quantitative parameters of the sump geometry that can be used in the interpretation of the observed results. The simulations performed in the article employ the macrosegregation model implemented on the platform of a commercial software CFX. The model includes the thermosolutal convection and shrinkage-induced ﬂows and does not include transport of solid grains (ﬂoating crystals) and deformation. The macrosegregation model consists of three parts: a macroscopic transfer model, a microscopic model that describes the solidiﬁcation path and relates the temperature to the phase fractions and compositions, and a phase diagram calculation module based on tabulation technique. The macroscopic transfer model was originally formulated by Bennon and Incropera[10] and reassessed by Prescott and Incropera.[11] The microscopic model is of the Scheil–Gulliver Table II. negative positive positive depends on the mold design positive (exudation) type. The complete model is described in the previous article,[7] and its details are not the subject of this article. Four simulations with diﬀerent billet dimensions and casting speeds were performed, as listed in Table II. The cylindrical shape of the billet allows us to use an axisymmetric two-dimensional (2-D) model. The 2-D calculation domain consisted of unstructured meshes in the center and structured mesh at the surface of the billet, which from our experience helps to avoid the numerical diﬀusion and dispersion that otherwise occur in macrosegregation calculations. Diﬀerent mesh size and heat transfer conditions are applied for diﬀerent characteristic cooling zones of the billet, i.e., hot top, mold, air gap, secondary, and downstream cooling. The length of these zones was predescribed from the geometry of the mold and casting.[7] The model does not include thermomechanical behavior of the solid; hence, the densities of solid and liquid are taken as constant and the air gap is not simulated. The air gap region is explicitly described by a lower heat transfer. Furthermore, we distinguish the parts of the billet below the impingement (secondary cooling) point with respect to the billet condition across its entire thickness. When the core is still liquid or semisolid, the ‘‘wall 1’’ condition with a ﬁner mesh is applied, whereas when the entire cross section is fully solid, the ‘‘wall 2’’ condition is enforced with a coarser mesh. The ﬁner mesh in wall 1 is required to resolve correctly both the solid and semiliquid parts of the billet. At the same time, the heattransfer conditions in these domains remain the same, because the cooling applied does not change. The mesh size used in each simulation is listed in Table II. The interpretation of the observations summarized in Table VI and by Eq. [2] agrees with the suggestions of Anoshkin and Dobatkin[9] that the direction and extent of macrosegregation are determined by a ratio between thermosolutal convection and shrinkage-induced ﬂow. This relationship, however, is not straightforward, and the ﬁnal macrosegregation pattern is a result of a fragile balance between the amount and composition of liquid that is brought to the center of the billet by the convective ﬂow and taken from the center by the shrinkage-induced ﬂow and the direction of relative movement of solid and liquid phases in the slurry zone. It is important that the transport of solid is assisted by the convection. Hence, ﬂoating crystals generally follow the direction of liquid ﬂow. At the same time, it is the 1210—VOLUME 39A, MAY 2008 relative movement of the solid and liquid (settling of the solid phase under gravity and ﬁltration of the solid through the slurry) that actually aﬀects the macrosegregation. At the end, the ratio between positive (normal) contribution of thermosolutal convection and negative contribution of ﬂoating crystals can be such that (1) both processes compensate for each other and the negative centerline segregation results from the shrinkage-induced ﬂow, (2) thermosolutal convection prevails and the segregation pattern depends on the ratio between convective and shrinkage contributions, and (3) ﬂoating crystals intensely accumulate in the center enhancing the negative segregation caused by solidiﬁcation shrinkage. The scaling dependence in Figure 2 and corresponding sump parameters in Table VI show that the increased casting speed at the same billet diameter will result in a deeper sump with a steeper solidiﬁcation front and in the transition from positive to negative centerline segregation. These observations are in line with our experimental results on the crucial role of the sump depth on the extent of the macrosegregation.[19] The depth of the sump is related to the slope of the solidiﬁcation front that is quantiﬁed in this work as the inclination angle A (Figure 4(a), Table VI, Eq. [2]). It is well know that the shrinkage-induced ﬂow tends to draw the solute-rich liquid deep into the mushy zone from the center to the billet periphery.[6,8] Recently, we have shown analytically that the extent of the negative segregation stimulated by the shrinkage-induced ﬂow is a function of the slope of the solidiﬁcation front.[21] So it does not come as a surprise that the steeper the solidiﬁcation front (smaller angle A in Table VI and Figure 4(a)), the greater is the extent of the negative segregation. The analysis of the same scaling dependence in Figure 2 tells us that for a given casting speed, a larger billet is more prone to negative centerline segregation. A large billet usually means a slower cooling, deeper sump, and a thicker transition region. The thicker transition region implies a thicker slurry zone. A higher ratio between the thickness of the transition region and the billet radius suggests a deeper sump. There are several correlated phenomena that can aﬀect the macrosegregation. First of all, the deep sump enhances the shrinkage-induced ﬂow and the negative centerline segregation, as we discussed in the previous paragraph. At the same time, the large slurry zone with loose solid crystals suspended and ﬂoating in the liquid matrix gives more opportunities of the thermosolutal convection ﬂow to penetrate into the transition zone and to wash out solute-rich liquid from it (Figure 4(b)). This liquid can go up and mix with the bulk melt or can be transported toward the center along the liquidus isotherm and eﬀectively facilitate the positive segregation. This process is assisted by grain reﬁning, when the solid phase becomes coherent at lower temperatures (higher fractions of solid). Hence, the eﬀect of the extent of the transition region is a function of the direction of thermosolutal ﬂow in the slurry zone. In addition, the ‘‘lose’’ slurry zone creates more opportunities for the ﬂoating crystals to be generated. Unfortunately, the actual casting speeds that may result in positive or negligible macrosegregation are much lower than those that are currently used in industry. IV. CONCLUSIONS 1. The macrosegregation model that takes into account the thermosolutal convection, shrinkageVOLUME 39A, MAY 2008—1211 induced ﬂow, and solidiﬁcation path of the alloy give computer-simulation results that agree well with a large set of reference data. 2. The scaling analysis performed in this article conﬁrms, based on the experimental and numerical results, that in a wide range of casting speeds and billet dimensions, it is possible to choose the casting conditions that produce negligible, or even normal, macrosegregation. Grain-reﬁned materials may be less prone to negative segregation than coarsegrained alloys. 3. A complex ratio of the thermosolutal convection that potentially can bring solute to the center of the billet and the shrinkage-induced ﬂow and transport of ﬂoating crystals that contribute to the negative centerline segregation gives the overall macrosegregation pattern. On the scale of the billet, the geometry of the transition region characterized by its thickness and the slope of the solidiﬁcation front plays a decisive role in the ﬁnal macrosegregation.
