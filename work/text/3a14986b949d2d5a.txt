However, it is still lack of investigation on how these interfaces may be tuned to enhance the mechanical properties. Herein, the multi-layer diamond coatings with different modulation periods (ʌ = 1.70, 0.87, 0.29 μm), containing microcrystalline diamond (MCD), sub-microcrystalline diamond (SMCD) and nanocrystalline diamond (NCD) as sub-layers, were synthetized by hot filament chemical vapor deposition (HFCVD). The interfacial microstructures were characterized by scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM). The hardness (H) and Young's modulus (E) of the coatings were evaluated by nanoindentation tests with two different modes. The planar defects including stacking faults (SFs) and twinning boundaries (TBs) are observed at both MCD/SMCD and NCD/MCD interfaces but absent at SMCD/NCD interfaces. Owing to more interfaces, SFs and TBs as barriers inhibiting the dislocation motion effectively, the H and E of the coatings are enhanced with the decrease of ʌ. Hence, the coating with ʌ of 0.29 μm, possessing more SFs, TBs and more sharp interfaces, obtains the highest hardness (78.39 ± 1.10 GPa). In summary, the mechanical properties of the multi-layer diamond coatings can be greatly improved by the elaborately designed interfacial microstructure, which is of great significance to promote the far-ranging application of diamond coatings. 1. Introduction The diamond coatings have been widely used in the cutting tools because of their extreme hardness, superior adhesive strength and favorable wear resistance [1–3]. The microcrystalline diamond (MCD) coatings possess superior wear resistance and favorable adhesive strength, but their columnar structure and coarse crystals usually result in the high surface roughness and thus a high friction coefficient, limiting the applications for the cutting tools and tribological contacts [4,5]. In contrast, the nanocrystalline diamond (NCD) coatings have dense microstructures and smooth surfaces [6], but they may weaken the mechanical interlocking effect between the coating and the substrate [7,8]. In order to combine their individual benefits, the multi-layer diamond coatings with alternating MCD and NCD layers have been successfully prepared [9–13]. More promising mechanical and tribo­ logical properties can be achieved in the multi-layer diamond coatings. The surface roughness is reduced due to the smaller crystalline size [9,10]. The residual stress become more homogeneously distributed because the alternative MCD and NCD layers may help to release the accumulated stress in each layer [11]. After indentation testing, the multi-layer diamond coatings display no visible delamination except for a few radial cracks and a limited extension of the crater area, which results from the multi-layer structure preventing the crack propagation and the underlying MCD sub-layer providing a higher adhesion force [12]. The coatings have no detachment or formation of pits after the impaction, because the damage proceeds by gradual loosening of ma­ terial from the surface [13]. The toughness is usually enhanced by the crack deflection at interfaces between each sub-layer, interface delamination and crack tip blunting [18]. Moreover, the mi­ crostructures of the interfaces exert a significant influence on mechan­ ical properties of the multi-layer coatings. Chen et al. [19] investigated the microstructure of the TiN/AlN multi-layers with a Ti transition layer, and they found that the stacking faults were formed in the TiN layer, which was induced by the high-density stacking faults in the Ti transi­ tion layer. The presence of the stacking faults was beneficial to improve mechanical properties of the multi-layers. Major et al. [20] reported that the misfit dislocations at the interface in the TiN/CrN multi-layers, which could not only relax the strain at interfaces but also decrease the friction coefficient and wear volume. For the multi-layer diamond coating, some scientists have studied their interfacial microstructures. Almeida et al. [21] observed that a thin graphite-like layer was formed at the interface from MCD to NCD layer. Jiang et al. [22] reported that the lattice distortions appeared at the MCD/NCD interface. In 2018, Bo Yang et al. [23] simulated a nanotwinned diamond/c-BN multi-layered material with extremely high strength, which was ascribed to the twinning boundaries, stacking faults and the alternately changed chemical bond at the interface. Nevertheless, little research has been done on the effect of interfacial microstructure of multi-layer diamond coating on mechanical properties. In this work, the multi-layer diamond coatings with various modu­ lation periods (ʌ, the total thickness of the sub-layers with different crystalline structures in the coating [24]), consisting of MCD as the beginning layer, SMCD as the middle layer and NCD as the end layer, were fabricated by the hot filament chemical vapor deposition (HFCVD). The interfacial microstructures of these coatings were characterized and analyzed by scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM). The hardness (H) and Young's modulus (E) of these coatings were accurately evaluated by the nanoindentation tests with two different modes, including the quasi-static load-controlled mode and the continuous stiffness measurement (CSM) mode. Then the effect of the interfacial microstructure on the nanoindentation hardness was comprehensively discussed. This work will provide an available pathway to enhance the mechanical property for the multi-layer diamond coatings by modulated interfacial microstructure and offer new insights into the rational design of hard coatings. 2.2. Correspondingly, the ʌ of each coating is about 1.70, 0.87 and 0.29 μm, respectively. The Raman spectra and the fitting curves of the multi-layer diamond coatings are displayed in Fig. 2a. The sharp and narrow peak at 1332 cm− 1 indicates the natural diamond [29]. In addition, the small peak located at around 1250 cm− 1 also arises from the feature of the NCD coatings [31]. The D and G peaks are at around 1350 and 1560 cm− 1 owing to the disordered sp2-bonded carbon and graphite phases [30]. The XRD patterns of the multi-layer diamond coatings with different ʌ are illustrated in Fig. 2b. According to the standard card (PDF 060675), the diffraction peaks at ~ 43.9◦ and ~ 75.2◦ (2θ) represent the (111) and (220) diffraction facets of the diamond, respectively. The other diffraction peaks present the WC substrate, since X-ray can penetrate the thickness of the diamond coating over 100 μm [32]. As the peak of the (220) diamond facet is coincided with that of the (200) plane of the WC, the crystalline quality is estimated by the full width halfmaximum (FWHM) of the (111) diamond peak. For sample A, B and C, the value of the FWHM(111) is about 0.445, 0.519 and 0.781, respectively, suggesting that the crystallinity would be improved with the increase of ʌ. By decreasing ʌ, the growth cycle is inadequate and thereby the non-diamond phases increase at grain boundaries. As a result, the peak of the (111) diamond plane becomes broad and its in­ tensity slightly decreases, indicating that the crystalline size is refined. The phenomenon is also observed in other reported multi-layer coatings [33,34]. 3.2. Effect of interfacial microstructure on the hardness The hardness relies strongly on the creation and movement of dis­ locations [44]. The dislocation mediated plastic deformation has been successfully observed in diamond at room temperature [45]. Therefore, the hardness is directly related to the dislocation motion in the diamond [46]. Herein, based on the results of interfacial microstructures and nanoindentation results, the reasons for the enhanced hardness of multilayer diamond coatings are discussed in detail, and two factors are responsible for the hardness evolution, that is, the sharp interfaces and the planar defects at interfaces. Many studies have reported that the interfaces between different sub-layers are regarded as the effective barriers to prevent the disloca­ tion motion in the multi-layer coatings, thus generating the hardening effect [24,33,42,47–49]. Fig. 7a and b exhibit the STEM-HAADF mi­ crographs of the selected multi-layer diamond coatings with the ʌ of 1.70 and 0.29 μm, which show the obvious interfaces in the coatings. For the coating with the thick ʌ of 1.70 μm, the dislocation motion could take place in each sub-layer with a large thickness. As a result, the impeding effect of the interface as a barrier on dislocation motion is weakened or even absent, which results in relatively low H value [33]. However, for the coatings with the ʌ of 0.29 μm, the ʌ is thin enough so that some dislocations could exist throughout the thickness of sub-layers and they must cross the interfaces, thus there are almost no other dis­ locations driving the front dislocations to move through the interface. The result indicates that the higher loads are required to deform the multi-layer diamond coatings, which is confirmed by the H as a function of the decreasing ʌ [42]. Consequently, the multi-layer diamond coat­ ings with the ʌ of 0.29 μm have the higher H. Similar results were also found in other multi-layer hard coatings such as WB2/CrN [33], CrN/ Si3N4 [48], CrN/AlN [49] and CrN/ZrN [50]. A number of TBs and SFs are observed at the MCD/SMCD and NCD/ MCD interfaces as shown in Figs. 3 and 4. It is reported that, in the conventional materials, TBs have been demonstrated to play a key role in hardening effect, which is identical to that of the high-angle grain boundaries as barriers against dislocation motion [51,52]. As disloca­ tions move to TBs, they are impeded and then react with TBs [53]. Moreover, the SFs, due to extensive twinning, also have a stronger hardening effect because of their impediment to propagation of the dislocations [53]. In 2014, Quan Huang et al. [36] demonstrated that TBs and SFs were found in the nanotwinned diamond, resulting in the unprecedented hardness, with Vickers hardness up to ~200 GPa. Moreover, in 2018, Bo Yang et al. [23] found that the TBs and SFs with weak atomic layers could extremely enhance the strength in the simu­ lated nanotwinned diamond/cBN multi-layered material. Therefore, both TBs and SFs of the multi-layer diamond coatings are favorable for preventing the dislocation motion and thus improving the H of the coatings. This phenomenon is also observed in other materials, such as nanocrystalline (NC) Ti thin films [54], NC-Pd films [55], Ag/Al multilayer films [56], Al/Mg multilayer films [57], nanostructured Ni3Al [53], boron carbide (B4C) hard ceramic [58]. The corresponding schematic diagrams of the interfaces and planar defects impeding the dislocation motion for the multi-layer diamond coatings are shown in 3.3.2. Continuous stiffness measurement mode Continuous stiffness measurement (CSM) is also called the dynamic nanoindentation technique, in which a harmonic load with highfrequency and small-amplifier is added to the quasi-static measure­ ment throughout the testing process [27,41]. In CSM mode, the maximum displacement into surface keeps constant while the corre­ sponding load is variable. Figs. S3-S5 reveal the typical hardnessdisplacement and Young's modulus-displacement curves for the multilayer diamond coatings applied with different maximum indenter dis­ placements. Obviously, the data of each sample exhibit very well repeatability. Besides, the values of H and E for WC substrates are 21.98 GPa and 479.30 GPa (Fig. S6), respectively. Since the tendency of H and E for the diamond coatings present an apparent plateau region at a range of displacements and both H and E values are higher than those of WC substrates, it is suggested that the WC substrates have little influence on the measured H and E values. At the maximum displacement of 300 nm, H values rises from 60.03 to 72.70 GPa with the decreased ʌ. In the case of 400 nm displacement, H values is enhanced from 59.00 to 71.48 GPa as the ʌ decreases. At the maximum displacement of 500 nm, H increases from 58.59 to 72.06 GPa with the decreasing ʌ. Although H and E values present a similar varying tendency, their amplitude of variation is slightly different.
