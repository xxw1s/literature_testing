Based on the thermo-physical properties of Ni60A-25 % WC powder and the design of the double ellipsoidal heat source model, a 3D finite element model of double-layer laser cladding with two channels is established. The evolution of the temperature field and the residual stress field at different laser process parameters are investigated to evaluate the quality of the cladding layer. The impact of different laser cladding process parameters on the cladding dimensions is investigated. The dimensions of the simulated molten pool are analyzed by fitting a polynomial curve. The simulation results show that the laser power is proportional to the temperature, and the temperature growth rate of the coating is significantly higher than that of the substrate. The scanning speed is inversely proportional to the temperature. The maximum temperature of the cladding is 1.5 times the maximum temperature of the substrate. The temperature growth rate of the cladding layer is twice that of the substrate. Due to the asymmetry of the heat source in the multi-pass cladding process, the laser energy absorption rate is not the same on both sides of the melted layer. The double ellipsoidal heat source model has a larger diffusion range in the un-melted powder, resulting in asymmetry in the width direction of the cladding layer. The analysis of the residual stresses in the cladding layer shows that the residual compressive stresses in all paths increase with the increasing laser power. The normal x residual compressive stress in the coating and the maximum residual compressive stress occur at the end of the laser beam scan. The residual compressive stress in all paths decreases as the scanning speed increases. The results show that the dimensions of the molten pool are proportional to the laser power and inversely proportional to the scanning speed. The cladding geometry can be calculated with polynomial fitting equations at a selected laser power (500 W–2800 W) and scanning speed (1 mm/s–8 mm/s). The error analysis of the simulation and the experimental results can validate the proposed finite element simulation model. The error in the molten pool size is less than 20 % for the simulation and experimental results. 1. Introduction Laser cladding is a new laser surface modification technology. By adding different composite powders, the surface properties of the matrix can be greatly improved by laser cladding. A laser cladding layer has the advantages of controlled heat input, high wear resistance, low distor­ tion, a small heat-affected zone, and a low dilution rate [1–3]. Conse­ quently, laser cladding has been introduced in the design of conductor damage tolerance in aerospace, the automotive and tool manufacturing industries, and food processing and medical applications [4–6]. Laser cladding has a high cooling rate, which leads to high residual stress generation. As a result, cracks appear in the cladding layer and reduce the cladding quality. In recent years, with the development of com­ puters, many scholars have adopted the finite element method to simulate the process of laser cladding. Liu et al. [7] developed a 3D finite element model (FEM) to investigate the impact of dynamic preheating on the molten pool. The effect of dynamic preheating on the thermal behavior of multi-orbital and multi-layer laser cladding was explored by applying an additional heat source in a localized area in front of a moving melt pool. Thus, dynamic preheating helped to reduce the possibility of thermal stress and cracking. Sun et al. [8] developed a 3D finite element model to simulate the temperature and stress field distribution induced by laser cladding. The thermal gradient, melt pool configuration, and stress evolution were monitored and calculated using numerical methods. The results showed that the crack susceptibility of the coating was dependent on the residual stress as well as the micro-aggregation of Cr, Ni, and Fe elements. Liu et al. [9] investigated the temperature field evolution of monorail cladding layers deposited by a wide-beam laser. The effects of the process parameters on the molten pool size, temperature gradient G, cooling rate ε, solidifica­ tion rate R and G/R were analyzed. The results showed that the laser power and scanning speed had an influence on the evolution of the thermal and stress fields. The stresses were the highest in the laser melting coating-substrate interface zone, and the stress level on the top surface of the cladding layer was higher than that of the other paths. Zhang et al. [10] performed simulations of the temperature field and stress field of Fe–Mn–Si–Cr–Ni memory alloy single-rail and multi-rail fused layers using ANSYS. A circular point-type heat source model was used. From the results, it could be seen that the molten area of the Fe–Mn–Si–Cr–Ni shape-memory alloy coating with a circular spot for single laser cladding had a semi-spoon shape, and the maximum value of the residual stress in all directions appeared at the end of the laser scan. The repeated heating of the Fe–Mn–Si–Cr–Ni shape-memory alloy coating with circular spots by multi-track laser melting resulted in a peak coating temperature of 2600 ◦ C due to heat transfer. The coating stresses showed a tensile-compression-tension distribution. In addition to using finite element methods to simulate the laser cladding process, many scholars have investigated the effects of key process parameters on the properties of the cladding layer. Majumdar et al. [11] developed titanium-aluminum alloy composites with titanium silicide dispersion and investigated the performance of laser melting composite coatings with 5 wt % Si and 10 wt % Si added to Ti45Al5Nb0.5Si master alloy at different process parameters. The results showed that the addition of Si led to the formation of microcracks in the microstructure at different parameters. The optimization of the process parameters was required in order to form defect-free microstructures and improve hardness. Kumar et al. [12] investigated the cladding morphology, the microhardness of the subsurface layer, and the wear behavior of the cladding surface for different laser powers and percentage composition of aluminum nitride in a powder mixture. From the results, it is clear that the thickness of the cladding layer increases with the increase of the percentage composi­ tions of aluminum nitride, while the laser power has a negative effect on the thickness of the cladding layer after reaching a certain value. When the laser power was increased, the formation of slight cracks and voids could be observed. Furthermore, to study the influence of process parameters on coating properties, many scholars have introduced some methods of fitting analysis and machine learning into the field of laser cladding. Chen et al. [13] analyzed the effects of process parameters (laser power, scanning speed, preset powder thickness, laser spot diameter, and multitrack overlap rate) on the geometric characteristics (thickness, width, and height) and microhardness of ceramic coatings on Ti6Al4V substrates. A support vector machine (SVM) prediction model was developed to predict the geometry and microhardness of the coatings. The results showed that the prediction model based on the SVM method was able to predict the relationship between the cladding process parameters and the coating quality characteristics with high accuracy when the corre­ lation coefficient was greater than 0.94. Nabhani et al. [14] investigated the empirical-statistical relationships between the laser power, laser scanning speed, and geometric characteristics of a single-layer cladding track using linear regression methods. The results based on the corre­ lation coefficient (R greater than 0.9) and residual analysis showed that the empirical-statistical relationships were in good agreement with measurements of monolayer cladding orbits. Liu et al. [15] used a nonlinear fitting model to fit the relationship between single-track cladding (STC) process parameters and cross-sectional profile geome­ try parameters. Zhao et al. [16] used a backward regression method to analyze the relationship between process parameters (laser power, scanning speed, and powder feeding speed) and YCF104 cladding track geometry. The results showed that the scanning speed had the most significant effect on the height of the cladding track, while the laser power had a significant effect on the width and depth of the heataffected zone. In summary, the finite element method is a cost-effective method for investigating the evolution of temperature and stress fields during laser cladding. However, there is less literature on the temperature and stress fields of Ni60A-25 % WC composite coatings with multiple multilayers with different laser process parameters. Moreover, the previous studies mainly used Gaussian heat sources, which could not accurately simulate the heat source input during the actual laser cladding process. There­ fore, a 3D transient finite element model is developed to simulate the distribution of the temperature field and stress field as well as the cladding geometry of the Ni60A-25 % WC laser cladding composite coating. The thermo-physical property parameters of Ni60A-25 %WC powder and the double ellipsoidal heat source model are used as input, and the simulation of the laser cladding process is achieved by combining the elemental birth–death technique. The relationship be­ tween the laser power, the scanning speed, and the temperature of the molten layer is clarified. The stress distribution and evolution of the cladding layer with different technological parameters are revealed. And the residual stresses are measured experimentally. In addition, the variation of the cladding geometry with different technological param­ eters is investigated. The width and depth of the molten pool for selected process parameters can be predicted by fitting polynomial curves. Finally, the experimental measurements of the cladding dimensions with different laser cladding technological parameters are carried out, and error analysis is performed with the simulation results. 2. Finite element modeling 1 2 3 5 4 Before analyzing the thermal–mechanical coupling problem, thermal analysis is performed on the heat conduction problem, and the final temperature field distribution is obtained from the transient or steadystate variation of the temperature field. Mechanical analysis is the variation of the displacement field or stress field. The problem of ther­ mal stress is an interaction between the two physical domains of heat and stress. Therefore, this is a coupled field analysis problem. The simulation of the laser cladding process is usually considered as a coupled thermal–mechanical analysis [18,19] (CTMA). ANSYS provides two methods for analyzing thermal stresses: completely coupled ther­ mal–mechanical analysis [20,21] (CCTMA) and sequential coupled thermal–mechanical analysis [22–24] (SCTMA). The CCTMA method is designed to obtain the results of thermal analysis and structural stress analysis by directly utilizing the temperature and displacement freedom of coupled elements. The SCTMA method is a purely thermal analysis, and the resulting nodal temperatures are applied as body loads to the structural stress analysis. However, the CCTMA method suffers from non-convergence and a long computation time when the model is complex. Pan et al. [25] found that the CCTMA method took 38 times longer to calculate than the SCTMA approach in their study of the braking process of automotive disc brakes. Therefore, in this research, the transient temperature and thermal stress field of the dual-channel double-layer laser cladding layer during laser cladding are simulated by SCTMA. The flowchart of the SCTMA method for analyzing the temperature field and stress field of the laser cladding layer is shown in Fig. 4. The elemental birth–death technique [26,27] is used to accu­ rately simulate the powder laser cladding process with side-axis feeding. In the simulation model, load steps are set according to the moving speed of the laser heat source. Each unit is “killed” and “activated” ac­ cording to the load step in turn, which can achieve the effect of laser cladding composite coating from scratch during the simulation process. This makes the simulation closer to reality and improves the accuracy of the temperature field simulation data. Conclusion In this research, a 3D transient finite element model based on a sequentially coupled thermal–mechanical method is proposed. A double ellipsoidal heat source model is used as the heat source input for laser cladding. The evolutions of the temperature field and stress field of Ni60A-25 % WC laser cladding composite coatings are investigated. The variation pattern of the cladding geometry with different process pa­ rameters is investigated, and the dimensions of the molten pool are predicted. The accuracy of the model and simulation results are verified by corresponding experiments. The conclusions of this text are as follows: To investigate the effect of different process parameters on the ge­ ometry of the cladding layer, monitoring points are placed at the boundary of the molten pool. The boundary of the molten pool (whether it is below the melting point of Ni60A-25 % WC (1163 K)) is determined by monitoring the point temperature. The polynomial fits for the molten pool width Yw1 and depth Yd1 at different laser powers are shown in Fig. 28(a–b). The mean square errors are 0.049 and 0.015. The di­ mensions of the molten pool are influenced by the laser power, which is illustrated in Fig. 28(a). The width and depth of the molten pool are inversely proportional to the scanning speed, as observed in Fig. 28(b). The dimensions of the molten pool can be obtained at a selected laser power. (1) The increase in laser power leads to an increase in temperature, and the temperature increase rate of the cladding layer is higher than that of the substrate. When the scanning speed is 5 mm/s, the maximum temperature for a laser power of 2800 W is approximately twice as high as the maximum temperature for a laser power of 800 W. Additionally, the time to reach the maximum temperature of the first layer is 0.7 s, and the time to reach the maximum temperature of the second layer is 16.5 s. When the laser power is 2800 W and the scanning speed is 2 mm/ s, the maximum temperature of the cladding layer is 4926.9 K and the maximum temperature of the substrate is 3024.6 K. The scanning speed is inversely proportional to the temperature, and the increase in the scanning speed causes a decrease in the maximum temperature reduction rate of the cladding layer.
