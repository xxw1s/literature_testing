ARTICLE INFO AB STRACT Keywords: Perovskite Solar cell ML DFT Band gap energy Hybrid perovskites show promise as low-cost photovoltaics. However, achieving high efficiency necessitates careful optimization of their composition. Traditionally, exploring the vast compositional space is inefficient and time-consuming. This study demonstrates machine learning (ML) techniques to effectively and rapidly map the relationships between perovskite composition and critical band gap energy (𝐸𝑔 ), leading to the path of finding highly efficient perovskites for solar cell applications. Among these, Random Forest and Gradient Boosting Regression algorithms demonstrated exceptional predictive performance. Compositions with 𝐸𝑔 values between 1.2 and 2.0 eV were screened for further study. To validate data-driven approach, 𝐹 𝐴𝑃 𝑏(𝐵𝑟0.375 𝐼0.625 )3 was selected from the screened data and further studied through Density Functional Theory (DFT) and experimental methods. To the best of our knowledge, no previous studies have analyzed organic-inorganic halide perovskites in such depth, focusing on their crystal structure, stability (offering a new perspective on organic components), and 𝐸𝑔 . Since the introduction of hybrid perovskite (HP) materials [1] they have attracted significant attention in the photovoltaics research community due to their promising power conversion efficiencies [2–5]. Over the past decade, the power conversion efficiency (PCE) of perovskite solar cells (PSC) has increased from 2.2 % to over 26.7 % [6,7]. Since then, several reports on PSCs and reviews on these types of solar cells have been reported [8,9]. Perovskite materials typically have the chemical formula 𝐴𝐵𝐶3 , where 𝐴 is an organic or inorganic cation such as 𝐶𝐻3 𝑁𝐻3+ , 𝐶𝐻2 (𝑁𝐻2 )2+ , or 𝐶𝑠+ , 𝐵 is a divalent metal cation like 𝑃 𝑏+ , 2 or 𝑆𝑛+ and 𝐶 is a halide anion like iodide, bromide or chloride. This 2 hybrid organic-inorganic composition offers several advantages over traditional inorganic or organic solar cell materials. The exceptional electronic characteristics of halide perovskites are attributed to their low carrier trapping and recombination rates [10,11]. Methylammonium (MA), formamidinium (FA) and mixed MA/FA cations are commonly utilized as 𝐴-site cation due to their favorable structural interactions within the perovskite lattice. While progress has been remarkable, several challenges must still be addressed to enable widespread commercialization of perovskite photovoltaics. The most challenging issue is operational stability, as HP can degrade when exposed to heat [12], moisture [13,14], oxygen [15,16], and UV illumination [12,17] over time [18–20]. So far reported, the maximum stability achieved is 10,000 h (1 year) [21]. Optimizing key parameters, like suitable 𝐸𝑔 levels around 1.2–2 eV for maximum power output under the solar spectrum, also requires further investigation. The size and nature of the 𝐴-site cation play a crucial role in determining the structural stability and properties of the perovskite material. Substitution of different 𝐴-site cations can lead to variations in the crystal structure, lattice parameters, and stability of the perovskite phase. The Goldschmidt Tolerance factor (𝑇𝑓 ) [22,23] serves as an indicator of the stability of the perovskite structure, 𝑇𝑓 = (𝑟𝐴 + 2𝑟𝐶 )∕(𝑟𝐵 + 𝑟𝐶 ). Here, 𝑟𝐴 , 𝑟𝐵 , and 𝑟𝐶 represent the radii of the monovalent cation, divalent metal cation, and monovalent halide anion, respectively. An ideal 3D cubic perovskite structure is characterized by a 𝑇𝑓 value of 𝑇𝑓 = 1, with the material maintaining the cubic structure within the range of 0.9 ≤ 𝑇𝑓 ≤ 1. Recent experimental and theoretical investigations have revealed that perovskite absorbers like (𝐹 𝐴 − 𝐶𝑠)𝑃 𝑏𝐼3 exhibit a 𝑇𝑓 ranging from 0.94 to 0.98 [22]. Replacement of 𝑀𝐴 by 𝐹 𝐴 was reported by Stoumpos et al. to effectively lead to a red-shifted absorption onset (∼ 1.5 eV) compared to 𝑀𝐴𝑃 𝑏𝐼3 [20]. The choice of the 𝐶-site anion can also impact the crystal structure, stability, and electronic properties of the perovskite material. One of the strategies to enhance structural stability is substitution or mixing components on the 𝐴-site to stabilize the photoactive black perovskite polymorphs [24], while substitution or mixing components on the halide site can be used to tune the 𝐸𝑔 of these materials [25]. Up to date, only about 102 types of materials have been explored, a fraction of the potential candidates, estimated to be around 107 . Notably, none of the explored perovskites show promise as a stable and high-efficiency solar cell absorber at the same time. To obtain more accurate Xray Diffraction (XRD) pattern for the selected composite, the specific relaxation steps were performed using DFT. Following this initial assessment, a more comprehensive approach was adopted wherein full structural relaxation was performed. In this phase, the cubic shape constraint was changed, enabling the algorithm to adjust freely to achieve the lowest energy configuration. This relaxation process enhances the accuracy of the computed results, as it accounts for potential distortions within the perovskite structure that may occur during synthesis or in practical applications. The refined calculations provide a more realistic representation of the expected XRD patterns, facilitating a better comparison between theoretical predictions and experimental data. This approach ensures that the computational algorithms align closely with the actual structural characteristics of the HP composite under investigation. ML methods A dataset of published HP 𝐸𝑔 values and corresponding cation/anion compositions was compiled from the literature research. In total, 110 data points were gathered, encompassing different organic cations (MA, FA), inorganic metal cations (lead, tin) and halide anions (iodide, bromide, chloride). Each composition was specified by fractional ratios of the constituent ions rather than absolute stoichiometry to account for variability in reported formulations. 𝐸𝑔 values were extracted directly from the cited works or estimated from absorption spectra using standard techniques. The dataset was randomly divided into a training/validation set consisting of 99 samples and a test set of 11 samples used for the final evaluation of the algorithms. All composition features were normalized to have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one to account for differences in measurement scales prior to analysis. Seven ML regression algorithms were evaluated using Python scikit-learn: Linear Regression (LR), Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR), Random Forest Regression (RFR), and Gradient Boosting Regression (GBR), Decision Tree Regression (DTR), Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), Multilayer Perceptron Regression (MPR). All ML calculations were carried out on a CPU(M1 Pro SoC). A detailed description of the working principles of these algorithms can be found in the supplementary material (Appendix A). 2.4. Synthesis and film preparation methods The solvothermal method was used to synthesize the chosen perovskite, 𝐹 𝐴𝑃 𝑏𝐵𝑟1.125 𝐼1.875 . Calculation of 𝐸𝑔 for unknown cases: ML vs. DFT The trained ML algorithms were utilized to calculate the expected 𝐸𝑔 values for compositions beyond the range of the original dataset. This methodology enabled us to extrapolate and estimate 𝐸𝑔 for cases outside the known data range. To facilitate this, a total of 44, 000 unique perovskite compositions were generated to predict their 𝐸𝑔 . The cation options were included, that is 𝑀𝐴, 𝐹 𝐴, and cesium (𝐶𝑠) in varying proportions, while lead (𝑃 𝑏) served as the metal component. Bromine (𝐵𝑟), chlorine (𝐶𝑙), and iodine (𝐼) were incorporated as halides, paralleling the variations in the cation species. Following the calculation and validation tests, we opted to focus on the results predicted by RFR and GBR. From the generated data, we specifically selected compositions where the predicted bandgap energy fell within the preferred range of 1.6–2 eV, with a tolerance factor between 0.9 and 1, and prioritizing those containing organic components. For these selected materials, the convex hull energies per atom were calculated using DFT, and they were found to be in metastable states (further details can be found in the supplementary material: Appendix C.3). Consequently, for certain compositions, DFT-calculated 𝐸𝑔 values were derived, particularly focusing on simpler composites that contain only FA as the cation (see Table 4). The composites are filtered based on structure simplicity, 𝐸𝑔 values, tolerance factor values and energy values above convex Hull. For DFT calculations of mixed compositions, we constructed supercells with representative distributions of the mixed ions (A-site and C-site). For selected compositions, 10 symmetrically distinct configurations were generated using the Special Quasi-random Structure (SQS) separately for A-site cations and for C-site halide, which mimics possible random arrangements of ions for a given stoichiometry. We observed an average energy deviation of 1.02–2.561 meV/atom and a lattice constant deviation of 0.035–0.060 Å. Among these, the configuration with the lowest total energy after full structural relaxation was selected for property calculations. This approach is widely used for modeling disordered solid solutions and has been shown to capture the essential physics of mixed perovskite systems [42–44]. As it is described above, convex hull analysis was performed using 0 𝐾 DFT total energies, following established high-throughput computational screening practices. And while such approach provides a useful first-order approximation for phase stability, they do not account for finite-temperature effects such as vibrational entropy or configurational disorder, which are particularly significant in hybrid perovskites. Therefore, the convex hull results presented in Table C.1 should be interpreted as a relative screening tool rather than a definitive measure of synthesizability. Conclusions In conclusion, this study highlights the potential of ML algorithms in predicting the 𝐸𝑔 values of perovskite materials based on their composition. The trained ML algorithms demonstrated the ability to calculate expected 𝐸𝑔 values for compositions beyond the existing dataset, enabling advanced extrapolation and estimation techniques. By selecting cases falling within the preferred 𝐸𝑔 range, further validation using DFT calculations, experiments, or literature data can be conducted. The analysis highlighted the significant role of the 𝐶 component in determining the predicted 𝐸𝑔 . Especially, the overall radius formed by the constituent elements within the 𝐶 component and the specific ratios of the three components played crucial roles in determining the 𝐸𝑔 . Compositions rich in iodine (𝐼) exhibited a strong preference for the desired energy range, whereas the composition of the 𝐴 component demonstrated only marginal influence, primarily impacting stability considerations. The application of ML algorithms facilitated a more efficient and targeted selection process for identifying promising perovskite compositions that meet specific 𝐸𝑔 criteria. The ML-predicted 𝐸𝑔 values were validated through DFT calculations and subsequently confirmed via synthesis and experimental characterization. Specifically, for 𝐹 𝐴𝑃 𝑏(𝐵𝑟0.375 𝐼0.625 )3 , the 𝐸𝑔 value was predicted as 1.74 eV using the RFR and GBR ML algorithms, calculated as 1.70 eV or 1.73 eV (depending on added Hubbard potential) through the 𝐺𝐺𝐴+𝑈ℎ DFT method, and experimentally determined to be 1.74 eV. This remarkable consistency across all three approaches — ML, DFT, and experimental verification — demonstrates a precision of 0.01–0.04 eV. The findings provide valuable insights for designing and optimizing perovskite materials with specific 𝐸𝑔 , contributing to advancements in the field of materials science and renewable energy applications.
