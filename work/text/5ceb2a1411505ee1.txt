Introduction Seals by magnetic materials are a type of non-contact sealing techniques with a largely different principle and property compared to traditional mechanical seals. In these seals, a magnetic circuit is established with a magnetic source and magnetically conductive parts. Magnetic materials (usually ferroﬂuid [1], magnetorheological ﬂuid [2] or magnetic grease [3]) are attracted in the sealing gaps by magnetic forces, and form several liquid O-rings to seal. Particularly, ferroﬂuid seals (FFS) are a high-performance sealing method with unique advantages like zero leakage, long lifetime, low friction torque and a simple structure [4,5]. Therefore, they have been used in high demanding sealing occasions including crystal growing machines, disk drivers, rotary joints of robots, rotary bloody pumps and so on [6–9]. Particularly, recent studies have showed great progress about FFS in large and medium-size advanced machines. For example, Urreta et al. [10] developed a hybrid journal bearing of active lubrication by magnetic ﬂuids. The hydrodynamic effect was improved by about half, and quasi-inﬁnite static stiffness was realized within a load range. In another research [11], they achieved high performance seals for high precision spindles of machine tools by a ferroﬂuid of proper sealing capability and low friction. Díaz-Tena et al. [12] designed a magnetorheological ﬂuid shock absorber to reduce the vibration during the milling process ⁎ Corresponding author. E-mail address: lidecai@mail.tsinghua.edu.cn (D. [13] designed a semi-active magnetorheological damper device to suppress chatter for the milling of thin-ﬂoor components. These studies proved signiﬁcant performance enhancement of advanced and high-precision machines by ferroﬂuids. However, limited by the properties of the carrier ﬂuid, FFSs cannot work under extreme temperatures. As the temperature elevates, the carrier ﬂuid of ferroﬂuid evaporates rapidly, then a leakage occurs. Under a low temperature (for example 210 K), the viscosity of the carrier ﬂuid becomes quite large [14], leading to a large starting torque of FFSs. Meanwhile, the pressure capability of an FFS per stage is approximately 10 kPa [15]. In order to resist a high pressure difference, tens of stages are often used in an FFS unit. As a result, the whole seal unit becomes heavy and occupies a large axial space. Besides, the lifetime of FFS depends largely on the working environment. On the one hand, it is long and stable in a moderate and proper working condition. Some earlyyear applications showed a lifetime as long as ten years. On the other hand, FFSs may come to failure quickly in extreme conditions like high temperatures, high rotary speeds or ultrahigh vacuum. An overlarge starting torque after long-time static placement can also cause the burning of the motor. These problems prevent FFSs from further engineering applications. Magnetic powders mainly include iron oxide, ferrite, rare earth alloys and so on. However, current applications of magnetic powders mainly focus on areas like powder metallurgy, water puriﬁcation, information storage, additive manufacturing, etc. [16–20] Recently, a novel sealing method using nano-mirco magnetic powders has been raised up to solve the problems in FFSs [21]. Pole tooth optimization As important parts of an MPS structure, the geometric parameters of pole teeth have a dominant inﬂuence on the capacity coefﬁcient in the sealing gaps, and further on the pressure capability of the MPS. In the study of FFSs, the optimization objective is the largest difference of magnetic ﬁeld intensity Hmax-Hmin. Although the optimizing process of FFSs has been widely studied by former researchers, most of them focus on one single parameter as the variable at a time and then change the variable one by one [3,32,33]. This method could enhance the pressure capability, but it ignores the joint inﬂuence on the optimization objective by difference parameters of pole teeth. For example, the optimized width value of pole teeth at a certain height of pole teeth is usually not the best at another height. So it cannot generate an optimal parameter set in pole tooth design. This problem is even more serious in pole tooth optimization of MPS, since the capability coefﬁcient as the optimization objective has a far more complex source of determinants. Here we realize the maximization of MPS pressure capability by derivative-free multiparameter optimization algorithms based on the objective function derived above. Derivative-free optimization is one class of optimization algorithms where the gradient of the objective function by control variables is not used [34]. Instead of following the gradient descent direction of the objective to search for the next improved set of control variables, they calculate the objective function at different sampling points in the control variable space. These algorithms are computationally more consuming than gradient-based ones, but the robustness is better. Particularly it is suitable in this case for the capacity coefﬁcient, of which the derivative can hardly be resolved analytically. During the optimizing process, key geometric parameters of pole teeth are selected as control variables. MPS experiment setup To test the validity and accuracy of the pressure capability formula proposed above, a sealing device for magnetic powders was manufactured and a test bench was established. As is shown in Fig. 7, key components of the sealing device include a shaft, two pole pieces, a shell with two deep-groove ball bearings inside, an inner sleeve, a sleeve and an end cover. Three pairs of pole pieces are made. Taking leakage rates into consideration, two of them have one pole tooth, with Lh = 2 mm, Lt = 1.5 and 2 mm, respectively. One pair has ﬁve pole teeth, with Lh = 2 mm, Lt = 2 mm and Lm = 1.5 mm. Fig. 8 indicates the set-up of the MPS test bench. During each sealing experiment, a certain amount of magnetic powders are ﬁlled in the sealing gaps, and the air in the gas chamber is compressed slowly by the air compressor. The pressure in the gas chamber and between two pole pieces is monitored by the computer and the critical pressure before a sudden pressure drop occurs is recorded as the pressure capability. Simplex search method Simplex search (SS) method is ﬁrst raised up by Nelder and Mead in 1965 [36], and then improved continuously by later researchers [37,38]. This direct search method is based on the transformation of a simplex, which is a convex polytope of n + 1 vortices in n dimensions. Given an initial simplex in the control variable space, the objective function values of all vertices are calculated. They are ordered according to their values f ðx1 Þ≤f ðx2 Þ≤⋯≤f ðxnþ1 Þ ð20Þ Then the vertex with the largest value is replaced by a better vertex generated by several transformation methods. For example, by reﬂection, the centroid xO of x1 to xn is calculated, and the reﬂected point xr = xO + α(xO − xn+1) with a reﬂection coefﬁcient α. If the reﬂected point has an improved objective value, the worst vertex xn+1 will be replaced. Other transformation includes expansion, contraction and shrink. A new simplex is created in each iteration until the convergence criterion is reached. 5. Results and discussion 5.1. Necessity of multiparameter optimization As is mentioned above, in the pole tooth design of FFSs, most researchers are used to optimizing one single parameter at a time and change the variable one by one. Here we examine the necessity of multiparameter optimization method for the optimal parameter set. Fig. 10 shows the optimization results with the width, height and interval of pole teeth one by one. The initial parameter set is the same as 5.1. The optimal single parameter is used as the preset condition in calculation of the next parameter. The results are Lt = 0.50 mm, Lh = 2.64 mm and Lm = 0.73 mm, and the optimized objective value is 266.21 kPa. The maximum objective value is 2.9% lower than that by multiparameter optimization method. This proves a joint inﬂuence on the capacity coefﬁcient by three geometric parameters of pole teeth, and the “one-by-one” optimization method cannot acquire a global optimum. For a maximum pressure capability, a multiparameter algorithm is necessary in MPS pole tooth design. SS moves rather slowly towards the stationary point in the early stage. Meanwhile, the convergence is stable yet time-consuming near the stationary point. In comparison, the objective function value rises in a fast and stable way by BOBYQA optimization. Moreover, the convergence near the stationary point is straightforward, with little accuracy loss of the maximum objective value. The magnetic powders in sealing gaps are assumed to be affected only by pressure and magnetic force, but in fact frictional forces exist between magnetic powders and pole teeth, and between magnetic powders and the inner sleeve. Depending on the magnetic ﬁeld strength and friction coefﬁcient of the powders, the friction can be quite large. Besides, the interparticle forces among magnetic nanoparticles are also complex. In addition, the magnetic powders are simpliﬁed to be cylindrical beneath the pole teeth, but the actual surface shape of magnetic powders is determined by a combination of forces. As a result, the principle of virtual work and subsequently the capacity coefﬁcient cannot describe the status of magnetic powders accurately. Another main source of error could be the dynamic change of magnetic powders during the pressure rising process. When the air on one side is compressed from atmospheric pressure to critical pressure, magnetic powders are pushed from the middle position to the position of the largest pressure capability. Consequently, the properties such as shape, density, magnetic induction keep changing. But the theory and simulation above only focus on and solve the static state, which is likely to receive an overlarge prediction of pressure capability. Meanwhile, the inﬂuences of experimental devices and procedures are non-ignorable. A key problem is how to load the magnetic powders uniformly in sealing gaps. Due to the poor mobility of powders, a sealing failure tends to occur where magnetic powders are not tightly ﬁlled. Besides, the size error of manufacturing and decentration of the shaft by assembling also leads to deviations. Conclusions In this research, we give a theoretical analysis of pressure capability of newly-raised magnetic powder seals, and realize the pole tooth optimization design. The formula of MPS pressure capability is derived based on the principle of virtual work. The capability coefﬁcient is put forward as a determinant coefﬁcient in structure design and optimization. Four derivative-free multiparameter optimization algorithms are used for the optimization of pole tooth structures. The inﬂuences by each geometric parameter on pressure capability are discussed. The characteristic of MPSs and FFSs is compared. Finally, a test bench for MPSs is established and the differences between experimental and simulation results are analyzed.
