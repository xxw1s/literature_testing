Incecik Shaped charges are widely employed in both military and civilian applications owing to their strong penetration capacities, such as weapon production, oil exploitation, mining, and tunneling. To augment the armor-breaking capability of a shaped charge, it is of utmost significance to enhance the velocity of the metal jet. Therefore, the hypervelocity shaped charge (HSC) has been proposed as a potential solution. Compared with ordinary shaped charges, HSCs can cause severer damage to the target owing to the faster velocity of its jet head. The Eulerian Finite Element Method (EFEM) is developed to build the axisymmetric numerical model, and it can simulate shockwave propagation, jet penetration, and target destruction. The model is validated by the experiments, and the convergence analysis is also performed. And the fundamental characteristics of HSC in underwater explosions including the metal jet and shockwave are investigated. Subsequently, the HSCs with the different liner trun­ cation heights HL and liner materials are simulated to obtain the optimal parameter (range) for penetration capacity. Analyzing the numerical results, when 8mm ≤ HL ≤ 13mm or the liner is made of aluminum, the metal jet generated by HSC will have a greater penetration capacity to the steel plate through the comparison of the crevasse diameter and residual velocity. Introduction In practical applications, the shaped charge holds pivotal importance in the areas of national defense and economic development. The shaped charge has the capability to generate a high-velocity metal jet that can travel at speeds ranging from 3 to 10 km/s, leading to substantial damage on neighboring structures. As a noteworthy illustration, the shaped charge indisputably excels in breaking through solid barriers and geological fortifications, leading to its extensive application in a diverse array of weapon warheads such as torpedoes, missiles, anti-tank rounds, and others (Zhang et al., 2022; FU et al., 2014; Iyer, 1999). Moreover, the application of the shaped charge within the petroleum industry is also considerable, specifically in the oil drilling (Jin and Zhang, 2020; Liu et al., 2019a; Li et al., 2010). In addition to the above applications, the shaped charge can still be used in ice breaking (Kolpakov et al., 2018), building demolition (Walters and Ground, 2003), or rock blasting (Yin et al., 2021). With their varied application possibilities, shaped charges have been extensively researched using theoretical, experi­ mental, and numerical methods. Compared with other methods, the numerical simulation demonstrates the advantages of being more cost-effective and timeefficient. In recent decades, various numerical methods have been introduced to simulate shaped charges. For instance, Chen et al. (Chen and Liu, 2012) proposed an Eulerian algorithm to simulate the forma­ tion of a metal jet, and the numerical results exhibited a high degree of concurrence with the experimental data. Furthermore, due to its capa­ bility of precisely capturing intricate boundaries and material de­ formations, the SPH method is prevalent in simulating fluid-structure interaction problems (Zhang et al., 2017a; Huang et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2017; Salehizadeh and Shafiei, 2022). Zhang et al. (2017b) employed a modified SPH method to examine the underwater explosion of shaped charges. Through their rigorous investigation, the impacts of diverse explosives and liner materials are evaluated, and the ideal parameters are ultimately determined. Based on previous research, it is apparent that regardless of the numerical method employed, one of the primary goals of numerical investigations is to identify the optimal design configuration for shaped charges. Typically, a shaped charge with a basic construction comprises a detonator, high-explosive material, casing, and liner. In the review study of shaped charges (Mehmannavaz et al., 2021), the advanced shaped charges include annular shaped charges, bimetallic shaped charges, hypervelocity sha­ ped charges (HSCs), and so on. Compared with other shaped charges, HSCs demonstrate a higher velocity of the jet head and stronger pene­ tration capacity towards the target. However, despite these unique properties, recent research on HSCs has been relatively scarce. Regarding the study of HSC, Minin et al. (2012) introduced the original concept of HSC and simulated the jet formation process of HSC. Afterward, Xu et al. (2019) evaluated the impact of distinct slab mate­ rials on jet damage to a steel target. Upon scrutinizing the experimental and simulation outcomes, they discovered that the HSC induced a greater penetration depth when the additional slab was made of tung­ sten. Further, Xu et al. (2020) conducted a study on metal jets produced by HSCs with varying slab thicknesses and their penetration into con­ crete targets. Their findings suggest that the maximum jet penetration depth occurs when the slab thickness is 7.5 mm, as determined through experimental means. Although the preceding research by these scholars has proven to be productive and informative in advancing the study of HSC, it is noteworthy that their investigations were confined to land explosions and did not encompass the underwater explosion of HSC. On the other hand, numerous scholars have conducted comprehensive research on underwater explosions, covering theoretical, numerical, and experimental aspects (Zhang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022; Li et al., 2019, 2023), resulting in significant achievements. However, these studies have frequently not considered the impact of metal jets generated by the shaped charges in underwater explosions. Therefore, the study of HSC underwater explosions is deemed to be of great significance. Consequently, the EFEM with the VOF method is developed for simulating the underwater explosion of HSC. The EFEM was initially proposed by Noh (1963), and its main framework was established and refined by Benson, 1992a, 1992b. Despite being infrequently employed in shaped charge investigations, the algorithm has proven to be a valuable tool in exploring underwater explosions and has yielded sub­ stantial advancements (Xu et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2021), recently. The content of the study is arranged as follows: the theoretical equations related to EFEM are initially introduced, together with the establishment of the numerical model. Afterward, the numerical model has been meticulously validated through a comprehensive comparison with experimental data, including validating the shockwave load and metal jet penetration. The convergence analysis of the present model was also carried out by taking different mesh sizes in this paper. Based on the established numerical model, typical features of HSC underwater explosions are ultimately studied and compared with those of ordinary conical shaped charge (CSC) underwater explosions. Besides, numerical results of metal jet penetration to the target under various liner trun­ cation heights and liner materials are discussed in detail, providing critical conclusions.
