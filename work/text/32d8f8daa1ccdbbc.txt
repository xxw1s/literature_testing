The characteristics and performance of the coating and the substrate including micro­ structure, phase constitution and hardness were probed by combined use of multiple characterization methods. Results show that fine lamellar grains are formed in the coating during the ultrafast non-equilibrium solidifi­ cation process induced by pulsed laser cladding. The average size of these grains is about 0.79 μm. The hardness test shows that the hardness of the coating is 433.82 HV, 30.4% higher than that of the Ti-6Al-4V substrate. Besides, a lower wear volume compared to the substrate is found for the coating which indicates greatly improved wear resistance. In-depth analyses suggest that the improved surface performance can be mainly attributed to grain refinement strengthening in the cladding zone. 1. Introduction Ti-6Al-4V alloy is an α + β dual-phase alloy with advantages such as low density and high-temperature resistance [1,2]. Since the United States successfully prepared the alloy in 1953, it has become the trump card in the titanium alloy industry for its good comprehensive me­ chanical properties. It is also the first titanium alloy to be applied in aviation field in China [3]. With the continuous improvement of the quality of Ti-6Al-4V alloy castings, it is widely used to manufacture aircraft parts [4]. However, in the process of casting and long-term hightemperature service, aero engine parts are prone to defects such as holes and cracks, leading to performance degradation of blades made of Ti6Al-4V alloy [5]. Such damages will have a serious impact on the flight safety of military and civil aviation, and even cause significant economic losses and casualties. This limits the application and promo­ tion of Ti-6Al-4V alloy to a large extent, so it is of great significance to modify the surface of Ti-6Al-4V alloy. Laser cladding is a surface modification technique with high pro­ cessing efficiency. Alloy powders with different compositions and properties can be rapidly solidified with the substrate surface using high energy density laser beam, which can improve the microstructure and properties of the substrate material [6,7]. According to Adesina et al. [8], the corrosion resistance of Ti-6Al-4V with laser-clad coatings was improved. Wang et al. [9] found that the hardness in the melting zone of pure titanium sheet after laser surface alloying treatment increased significantly. According to Mthisi [10], the 90Ti-10Al2O3, 90Ti-8Al2O32Zn and 90Ti-4Al2O3-6Zn coatings were deposited onto Ti-6Al-4V alloy via laser cladding. The 90Ti-10Al2O3 coating yielded optimal micro­ hardness along with maximal wear resistance. According to Phala [11], Ti–Si binary coatings were cladded on Ti-6Al-4V alloy to improve the surface properties of the alloy. They also found that the microhardness was improved owing to the presence of Ti5Si3 and TiSi2. According to Zhang et al. [12], mixed powder of Ti and Al were in-situ synthesized on Ti-6Al-4V substrate by laser cladding. The wear rate of composite coating was lower than Ti-6Al-4V substrate at elevated temperatures. Dai et al. [13] found that the hardness of Ti-6Al-4V hot-rolled sheet increased significantly after laser surface treatment. According to Zhao et al. [14], the mechanical properties of titanium oxide coatings deposited on Ti-6Al-4V alloy via laser cladding were improved. The secondary phases and grain boundaries effected hardness of the oxide coatings which was approximately 4 times as high as that of the sub­ strate. In general, the results show that laser cladding technique can effec­ tively improve the surface properties of materials, and has been widely used in the field of material surface modification. To make the cladding layer have better mechanical properties, it is necessary to ensure that the composition of the metal powder is as close as possible to the substrate metal so that the repaired area and the substrate metal area can form a good fusion. Note that there have to-date been very few attempts of preparing titanium (Ti) coatings on Ti–6Al–4V alloys by laser cladding. In this work, Ti coating was prepared by laser cladding on the Ti-6Al-4V alloy. The microstructure, phase composition, microhardness, residual stress and wear resistance of the substrate and coating were probed by combined use of multiple characterization methods. Then the relation­ ship between microstructure and properties was studied, allowing the modification mechanism to be explored. Besides, an energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS) was utilized to analyze the coating’s local compositions. Before the above characterization tests, the surface of the sample was me­ chanically ground with 400–3000# SiC sandpaper and then electropolished in a mixture of 90 mL methanol and 10 mL perchloric acid at − 30 ◦ C and 14 V for 2 min. After polishing, the sample surface was washed with alcohol and dried. The hardness of Ti-6Al-4V substrate and coating after laser cladding was tested by digital microhardness tester (HVS-1000Z). Because the surface of the sample after cladding was rough, the surface of the sample was polished with 400–1200# sand­ paper before the test until there was no noticeable scratch, and then the hardness test was carried out under the condition of loading of 10 g and holding time of 10 s. One measurement point was made on the surface of the sample at 200 μm intervals along the straight-line measurement path. Such measurement was repeated at least 12 times. Then the maximum and minimum values were removed and the average hardness value was calculated. The X-ray diffraction residual stress measurements of the substrate and coating were performed using a Proto-LXRD appa­ ratus. More than three locations on the surface were detected along the measuring path at an interval of 10 mm and such measurements were repeated three times to calculate the average residual stress values. The wear tests were conducted using a linear reciprocating wear tester (MFT5000) at room temperature, with GCr15 balls (6 mm in diameter) selected as the worn pair. They were performed under an applied load of 10 N, the amplitude of 10 mm, frequency of 20 Hz and sliding time of 15 min. The wear volume losses and worn surfaces were examined by a 3D white light profiling system (Bruker Contour GT-K). 2. Materials and methods A Ti-6Al-4V sheet (Al 5.50–6.75 wt% and V 3.50–4.50 wt%, with Ti balanced) was used as the test substrate material with its size of 80 × 50 × 5 mm. The powder material was the powder of pure titanium metal (purity >99.9%). After polishing the Ti-6Al-4V sample, the titanium powder was mixed into paste by polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) organic sol­ vent, and then the coating with thickness of 1000–1200 μm was placed on the surface of the sample. Finally, the pre-coated samples were dried in a drying oven at 120 ◦ C for 8 h, and then the surface of the alloy samples was processed by pulsed laser (HWLW-600A-C-V5.2.01). The selected laser power was 300 W with corresponding pulse duration and frequency of 5 m s and 20 Hz. Other processing parameters include beam diameter of 1 mm and a scanning speed of 10 mm s− 1 with a 50% overlap between neighboring tracks. During the pulsed laser cladding, a continuous flow of high-purity argon gas (flow rate 10 L min− 1) was used to prevent oxidation. After the pulsed laser cladding treatment, the coating was subjected to X-ray diffraction (XRD, PANalytical Empyrean Series 2) examination with Cu Kα radiation (8.04 keV) at 40 kV and 40 mA (scanning angles range from 30◦ to 80◦ ). The jade6 was used for post-processing and qualitative analysis of crystal structure. Phase and microstructure 3.1.1. XRD Fig. 1 shows the XRD phase analysis results of the Ti-6Al-4V substrate and the coating. Two different phases in Ti-6Al-4V substrate in the range of 30–80◦ with 2θ can be found. The diffraction peaks of α-Ti are (10− 10), (0002), (10− 11), (10− 12), (11− 20), (10− 13), (20− 20) and (11− 22) from left to right respectively, while there are only three groups of diffraction peaks in β-Ti, namely (110) and (200). The number and intensity of diffraction peaks of α phase in the Ti-6Al-4V substrate are much higher than that of the β phase. Therefore, it is clear that the Ti6Al-4V substrate has a dual-phase microstructure with α-Ti as the main component and β-Ti as the auxiliary. EBSD Fig. 5 shows EBSD result of the cross section. It can be seen from Fig. 5(a) that the cross-section of the sample was composed of cladding zone, heat-affected zone, and substrate. The microstructure of heataffected zone is fine, while the substrate is coarse, consistent with the results of ECC. Fig. 5(b) shows that the orientation of the sample microstructure is relatively scattered. Fig. 5(c) shows that residual strains exist in all three regions under the action of rapid heating and cooling. The EBSD was also used to analyze the misorientation angle and the rotation axis distributions of the sample cross section, as shown in Fig. 5(d). The sample has misorientation angles predicted by the Burgers orientation relationship near 10◦ , 60◦ –63◦ , and 90◦ . Fig. 6 shows a closer EBSD characterization of the cladding zone. Orientations of the lath structure are scattered, most of which are separated by high angle grain boundaries (HABs). The above orientation characteristics can be related to the α → β → α phase transformation behavior in the cladding process. As shown in Fig. 6(c), the CZ’s KAM value is higher near low angle boundaries (LABs). For CZ, it is close to the laser beam to obtain sufficient heat and is completely melted in this area. The subsequent rapid cooling causes the initial structure to transform into martensite. In titanium alloys, the transformation be­ tween α and β phases usually follows the Burgers orientation relation­ ship. According to this relationship, six β variants with different orientations could be produced by one α orientation during the heating process, and 12 α variants with different orientations could be produced by one β orientation during cooling process [19]. The cladding zone underwent a phase change behavior of α → β → M (α) during the clad­ ding process. Therefore, an original α orientation can at most produce 72 different orientations, leading to the presence of various orientations in the CZ (Fig. 6(b)). In addition, according to Burgers OR, there are five possible misorientation relationships between α variants obtained from the same β parent phase transformation: 10.5◦ /<0001>, 60◦ /<11–20>, 60.8◦ /<34–71>, 63.3◦ /<55–103> and 90◦ /<34–70> [20]. The com­ parison shows that the two misorientation angles around 60◦ and 90◦ in CZ have good coincidence with the latter four Burgers misorientation angles. Fig. 7 shows the EBSD results of the heat-affected zone of the sample. It can be seen from the figure that the sample is composed of α grains with varying sizes and fine lath structure, consistent with the results of ECC in Fig. 4(c) and (d). The average width of lath is found to be about 0.65 μm, smaller than that in CZ. In Fig. 7(b), it can be found that there are almost no LABs in the bulk α grains. The reason is that the heat obtained by HAZ is large enough to cause recrystallization and grain growth of the original subgrains. Fig. 7(c) shows that HAZ’s KAM value is higher at LABs, suggesting higher strains. Fig. 7(d) is the distribution of misorientation angle and rotation axis corresponds. The HAZ of the sample has misorientation angle and characteristic rotation axis around 60◦ and 90◦ , similar to that in Fig. 6(d). There are still a few other misorientation angles in the HAZ, which might be related to the bulk α grains. EDS and ECC Fig. 2 shows the EDS line scan results, revealing that the coating thickness was about 390 μm. The distribution of all three elements (Ti, Al and V) in thickness direction is relatively uniform, which indicates that the precoating elements (Ti) and the substrate elements (Ti, Al and V) have been thoroughly mixed during the laser cladding process. Therefore, a good metallurgical bond is formed between the coating and the substrate. In Fig. 3(a), the substrate is mainly composed of α grains and residual β phase of black short-rod shape structure scattered at the α-grain boundary of substrate. The α grains are mainly composed of two different morphologies of α-phase, i.e., blocky α grains and acicular secondary α grains transformed from high temperature β-phase. It was found that the average size of the blocky α grains was about 4.7 μm. Conclusions Acknowledgments Pure titanium coatings are successfully prepared on Ti-6Al-4V alloy by laser cladding technique. A series of characterization techniques are used to study the surface microstructure and properties of the substrate and coating after cladding treatment. (1) After laser cladding treatment, there were two modified zones with a significant difference in microstructure characteristics in the coating. The microstructure of the cladding zone near the center of the laser beam is composed of fine martensite lath and disorderly distributed β phase. The heat-affected zone far away from the laser beam center is composed of untransformed α grains, β phase particles and lath martensite. (2) Compared with the substrate, the surface hardness and wear resistance of the coating is significantly increased. The reason for this phenomenon is the combination of fine grain strengthening, grain boundary strengthening and the second phase strength­ ening in the cladding zone. Therefore, laser cladding technique can effectively improve the surface performance of Ti-6Al-4V substrate. (3) Compared to the substrate, the average residual stresses on the surface of the cladding zone is higher.
