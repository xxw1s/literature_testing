A single-phase CrN layer with a grain size ranging from 20 to 50 nm and an underlying layer consisting of a-Fe and nanosized CrN grains with a grain size ranging from 40 to 80 nm were formed by a 24 h chromizing at 773 K. Keywords: Surface nanocrystallization; Chromium nitride; Low-temperature chromizing; Plasma nitriding Surface nanocrystallization can be realized in three ways [1]. The ﬁrst method is based on various surface coating and deposition processes [2,3]. The second method transforms the surface layers of materials into a nanocrystalline structure while keeping the overall composition unchanged [4,5]. The third method is a duplex process in which a nanostructured transformable layer is developed and joined with chemical, thermal or metallurgical processes to produce nanostructured layers with diﬀerent chemical compositions or diﬀerent phases [6]. The purpose of this discussion is to report a novel process with which a nanostructured CrN layer can be formed directly on a nitrided steel surface by a lowtemperature chromizing process. By this method, a tool steel (AISI H13) was surface nanocrystallized with CrN grains. The substrate material was a commercial AISI H13 steel containing 0.38C, 5.41Cr, 1.18Mo, 0.94Si, 0.93V, 0.23Mn, and balance Fe (in wt.%). The specimens were vacuum austenized at 1303 K for 1.5 h, oil quenched and tempered at 823 K for 3 h. The heat-treated specimens were then polished with up to 900 grade emery paper, degreased, cleaned and plasma nitrided at 813– 833 K for 5.5 h. All the specimens concerned were plasma nitrided together in a single batch to ensure an even nitrided layer throughout them. X-ray diﬀraction of the specimens were conducted using a PHILPS X’Pert MPO Pro X-ray diﬀractometer with Cu Ka radiation. Cross-sectional specimens were etched with 3% Nital and characterized using a LEO1530VP scanning electron microscope (SEM) with a quadrature back-scattering detector. The Cr Ka1 and Fe Ka1 proﬁles across the coatings were determined by X-ray energy-dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and that of N Ka was determined by electron probe microanalysis (EPMA). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of the cross-sectional foils was carried out with a JEM 2010 transmission electron microscope working at 200 kV. Here, sublayer C1 is a chromized compound layer consisting of single-phase CrN, sublayer D is a mixture of CrN and a-Fe, and sublayer C2 consists mainly of c0 -Fe4N. Sublayer (D + C2), also named the diﬀusional layer of chromizing, became a single layer D with the layer C2 having disappeared as the chromizing time was increased from 6 h (Fig. 1b) to 24 h (Fig. 1d). It was found that the chromized layer as a whole became thicker as the chromizing time was prolonged, thus leading to a chromized layer thickness larger than that of the original nitrided compound layer, whereas the chromized compound layer C1, having been formed quickly in the early stages of chromizing, remained nearly unchanged in thickness with increasing chromizing time. For example, the original nitrided compound layer C in Figure 1a, while splitting into the three sublayers C1, D and C2 during chromizing, was thickened from about 12 to 23 lm during a 24 h chromizing at 773 K, with the chromized compound layer C1 having a constant thickness of 3–4 lm. The XRD patterns in Figure 2 show that the intensity of the nitriding-formed e-Fe2N and c0 -Fe4N phases is decreased, while that of the CrN phase is increased, with prolonged chromizing duration, and that the e-Fe2N in the original nitrided compound was nearly completely consumed during a 6 h chromizing, with only c0 -Fe4N remaining, whereas both e-Fe2N and c0 -Fe4N were present in the as-nitrided compound layer. The partial pressure of N2 in equilibrium with e-Fe2N can be as high as 25 GPa at 843 K [9], thus promoting vacancy coalescence and hence pore formation within the e-Fe2N layer. Pore nucleation might be energetically favored at grain boundaries due to the presence of grain boundary energy, though the possibility of intragranular nucleation cannot be ruled out. The pores then grow and coalesce, forming channels along the grain boundaries [9], which might serve as a fast diﬀusion channel for the activated [Cr] atoms. The pore/channel formation process was revealed by the SEM results shown in Figure 1b, in which a porous nitride compound layer, marked as layer C2, developed after the specimen was chromized for 6 h. Layer C2 is morphologically diﬀerent from the dense nitriding-formed compound layer C in Figure 1a. Hence, the diﬀusivity of the [Cr] atoms was enhanced by diﬀusing along those formed channels, and a single sublayer D (in Fig. 1d) of about 19 lm thickness was formed by a 24 h chromizing at 773 K. With those formed channels, the [Cr] atoms quickly diﬀused inward and arrived at the C2 layer, where they were likely to react with the [N] released from the retained iron nitrides in C2 layer and hence form a sublayer consisting of CrN and a-Fe (layer D in Fig. 1b and d). Again, the CrN was formed via reaction (2), wherein the [N] atoms needed for the reaction could be produced via the following reaction, in addition to reaction (1) c0 -Fe4 N ! 4a-Fe þ ½N ð6Þ Reaction (6) was conﬁrmed by the XRD results shown in Figure 2, which reveal that a-Fe, in addition to CrN but without c0 -Fe4N, was detected in the specimen chromized for 24 h, in contrast to the as-nitrided specimen, in which both c0 -Fe4N and e-Fe2N but not a-Fe were detected. In view of the fact that no chromium nitrides other than CrN were detected after the 24 h chromizing process, the formation mechanism of CrN during chromizing is assumed to be following reaction (2), in which the [Cr] and [N] atoms available are combined to form CrN. According to Somers and Mittemeijer [13], the diﬀusion of nitrogen through c0 iron nitride is very slow compared with diﬀusion through e iron nitride, indicating that the additional [N] atoms released according to reaction (1) found it more diﬃcult to diﬀuse outward through the c0 zones (which resulted from reaction (1) during the early stage of formation of the C1 layer) to react with [Cr] atoms than to diﬀuse inward through the e zones to transform the adjacent interior substrate (previous supersaturated with nitrogen) into iron nitrides. This was conﬁrmed by the EPMA depth proﬁle of the N Ka across the chromized layer, indicating that the N content in the C2 layer (in Fig. 1b) is markedly higher than in the interior a-Fe substrate, with the XRD spectrum showing that only c0 iron nitride was detectable (Fig. 2). Hence, it can be seen in Fig. 1a and b that the iron nitrides compound layer was thickened by a 6 h chromizing, which yielded a complete chromized layer (C1 + D + C2) 22 lm in thickness, which is much thivker than that (12 lm) of the original nitrided compound layer (layer C in Fig. 1a). Also, measurements showed that the overall thickness (22 lm) of (C1 + D + C2) in Figure 1b was nearly equal to that (23 lm) of (C1 + D) in Figure 1d, indicating that, no matter what chromizing time was employed, the complete chromized layer was always conﬁned to within the iron nitrides compound layer. During chromizing, layer C1 was ﬁrst formed in the outer part of layer C, followed by the formation of layer D in the inner part, all relying on the formation and diﬀusion of the [N] and [Cr] atoms produced by reaction (1) or (6) and the [Cr] source respectively. Once formed, the layer D would grow inwardly, consuming the remaining portion of the iron nitrides compound layer (such as the layer C2 in Fig. 1b). Once the chromizing process has proceeded for a certain period of time, e.g. 24 h at 773 K, the remaining portion of the iron nitrides compound layer is completely consumed by layer D, thus generating a single, expanded layer D and a complete chromized layer consisting of layers C1 and D (Fig. 1d). According to the equation given by Sun and Bell [14], the changes in Gibbs free energy at 773 K for reaction (2) can be calculated to be 136 kJ mol1, thus indicating a strong thermodynamic tendency to form CrN. This high thermodynamic driving force which ensures a high CrN nucleation rate of CrN, and the low lattice diﬀusivity of Cr at the relatively low chromizing temperature that can retard the growth of CrN, may have created a condition for forming small, or even nanosized CrN grains, as has been observed. The TEM images and the inserted electron diﬀraction ring pattern (in Fig. 3a and b), taken from the compound layer C1 in Figure 1 using a cross-sectional foil, indicate that the layer indeed consisted of single-phase CrN having a grain size ranging from 20 to 50 nm. As shown in Figure 4, nanosized CrN grains were also found to have formed in the chromized diﬀusional layer (layer D in Fig. 1d). Figure 4 also shows the common situation wherein a large grain, such as the one marked ‘‘A”, is surrounded by a number of small grains, such as those arrowed. Judging from the corresponding selected area diﬀraction pattern inset at the lower right corner in Figure 4, the small grains were also nanosized CrN grains, with a grain size ranging from 40 to 80 nm. According to the selected area diﬀraction pattern inset at the upper left corner in Figure 4, on the other hand, the large ‘‘A” grain was conﬁrmed to be a body-centered cubic a-Fe grain containing as much as 90.24 wt.% Fe, as determined by EDS. It should be noted that the CrN grains formed here are morphologically and crystallographically markedly diﬀerent from those formed in the diﬀusional layer of nitrided H13 steel by the internal precipitation mechanism [15], thus conﬁrming that the nanosized CrN grains were formed during chromizing, not during nitriding. This conclusion is further evidenced by the Cr content of about 8.13 wt.% (determined by SEM/EDS) in layer D, which is higher than 5.41 wt.% Cr, the average Cr content of H13 steel. The a-Fe grains were again assumed to be formed via reaction (6). Speciﬁcally, the [N] atoms generated accompanying a-Fe formation by reaction (6) would participate in forming CrN with the incoming [Cr] atoms via reaction (2), thus leading to the formation of a large a-Fe grain surrounded by small CrN grains. Also, at such a low temperature the growth of the CrN grains was considerably retarded, hence the CrN grains formed remained nanosized, despite being annealed for 24 h during chromizing. In summary, a complete chromized layer, consisting of three sublayers, C1, D and C2, was formed within, and at the expense of, the iron nitrides compound layer by chromizing plasma-nitrided specimens at 773 K for 3–24 h. Here, sublayer C1 is a chromized compound layer consisting of single-phase CrN, sublayer D is a mixture of CrN and a-Fe, and sublayer C2 consists mainly of c0 -Fe4N. Layer C2 was completely consumed by the inwardly growing layer D, and a single, expanded layer D as well as a complete chromized layer (C1 + D) were formed when the chromizing process proceeded for a certain period of time. Dense, nanosized and single-phase CrN grains were formed in layer C1, whereas large-sized a-Fe grains surrounded by nanosized CrN grains formed in layer D. The [N] needed for forming CrN, and the formation of a-Fe, resulted from the decomposition of eFe2N and c0 -Fe4N. A high thermodynamic driving force for CrN nucleation and a low lattice diﬀusivity of Cr at the relatively low chromizing temperature, which could retard the growth of CrN, facilitated the formation of the nanosized CrN grains. Fast diﬀusion channels were formed along grain boundaries within the original nitrided compound layer due to the formation of N2 via the combination of [N] atoms; these channels are assumed to play an important role in facilitating [Cr] atom diﬀusion needed for CrN formation.
