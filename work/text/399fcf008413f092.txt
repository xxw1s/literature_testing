Superplasticity can be achieved through grain boundary sliding at elevated temperatures in high-alloyed Mg systems, with a typical grain size < 10 μm stabilized by high densities of intermetallic precipitates. However, it is difficult to stabilize small grains and facilitate grain boundary sliding in low-alloyed systems due to insufficient precipitates. Here, by utilizing a distinctive design strategy, i.e. introducing solute segregation to enhance stability of the fine-grained structure, we obtained an Mg-1Zn-0.2Ca-0.2Zr-0.1Ag (wt.%) alloy achieving ~450% superplastic strain. Through quasi-in-situ Electron Backscatter Diffraction analyses, we investigated systematically microstructure evolutions during superplastic deforma­ tion of the alloy at different strains. It has been found that superplasticity is achieved through the absorption of intragranular dislocations by grain boundaries, which are associated with stressdriven grain boundary migration mediated by disclination-dislocation reactions. By using a theoretical description based on stress-driven grain boundary migration and disclinationdislocation reactions, our model can capture multiple superplasticity mechanisms (e.g., grain boundary sliding and grain rotation), which establishes a fundamental relationship between the evolution of microstructural defects and the strain accommodation during superplastic defor­ mation. Our results not only suggest an effective way to achieve superplasticity in low-alloyed Mg systems, but also provide a new insight to elucidate the nature of superplasticity mechanism by revealing the intrinsic correlation between stress-induced grain boundary migration and dis­ clination/dislocation motion. 1. Introduction Magnesium is the lightest structural metal with a density of 1.74 g/cm3, about 35% and 77% less than that of aluminum and iron, respectively (Shi et al., 2017; Tam et al., 2021). Magnesium alloys are widely used to improve energy efficiency in automobile, aircraft, and aerospace applications (Figueiredo et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2001). On the other hand, superplasticity, i.e., the ability of a polycrystalline material to exhibit extremely large deformation at elevated temper­ atures (Zecevic et al., 2020), provides a promising avenue to enhance the ductility of Mg alloys and produce complex-shaped components. Stabilized fine-grained structure is a critical factor to achieve superplasticity in metals. The conventional strategy to stabilize finegrained structures is introducing a high density of second-phase precipitates, which impedes grain growth through Zener Pinning mechanism (Kim et al., 2017; Rong et al., 2018), as designed in Ti-6Al-4 V (Alabort et al., 2019), Mg-Al-Zn (Zha et al., 2017) and Al-Mg alloys (Meng et al., 2019). However, this method is not effective in low-alloyed Mg systems, due to the low alloy content and the lack of precipitates. We obtain superplasticity in a low-alloyed Mg system (contents of added elements < 2.0 wt.%) by introducing solute segregation, which not only improves the stability of fine-grained structures, but also reduces the cost of superplastic Mg-alloys. Using this strategy, we show that an Mg-1Zn-0.2Ca-0.2Zr-0.1Ag (wt.%) (ZXKQ1000) alloy, can reach ~450% superplastic strain at 300 ◦ C with an initial strain rate 1 × 10− 3 s − 1, as compared to ~300% of typical AZ31 alloys at the similar temperature and strain rate (Abu-Farha and Khraisheh, 2007a; Chaudry et al., 2021; del Valle et al., 2007; Zha et al., 2017). In the literature, it has been widely recognized that one of the primary superplasticity mechanisms is grain boundary sliding (GBS) mechanism (Pearson, 1934). However, grain translation (i.e., one grain translates relative to its neighboring grain at their boundary) should cause strain incompatibility at related triple or quadruple junctions, which requires dislocation-assist accommodation (Ashby and Verrall, 1973; Ball and Hutchison, 1969; Gifkins, 1976; Masuda et al., 2017, 2016). Based on previous experimental observations, several models have been proposed to describe dislocation motions associated with GBS. Ball and Hutchison (Ball and Hutchison, 1969) suggested that GBS could be accommodated by dislocation activities across the blocking grains in zinc alloys. Gifkins (1976) claimed that GBS should be accommodated by dislocation activities limited within the vicinities of grain boundaries but excluded from the grain centers, based on his studies of lead alloys. Ashby and Verrall (1973) proposed a model that the grain switching events could be achieved through local diffusional mass transport around triple junctions even without involving dislocation slip. Masuda et al. (2017, 2016) suggested that GBS was accommodated by transgranular dislocation activities initiating from triple junctions into grain interior in oxide-dispersion-strengthened ferritic steels. Although previous studies imply an intrinsic correlation between GBS and dislocation motion, there is no quantitative analysis about (i) the motion of grain boundary dislocations and intragranular dislocations and (ii) the type and density of dislocations associated with the change of grain boundary (GB) structure during GBS, partially due to the lack of a rigorous theoretical framework. In general, diversified stress-driven GB migration (SDGBM) mechanisms, e.g., GBS and grain rotation, can occur during superplastic deformation, which requires a new theoretical framework capturing the co-evolution of multiple types of defects. In this work, we investigate the mechanisms of superplasticity from a new perspective, i.e., the cooperative motions and reactions of dislocations and disclinations, which leads to a quantitative description of SDGBM based on the topological theory of defects (Kleman and Friedel, 2008; Mermin, 1979). Here we suggest a new superplasticity model, based on disclination-dislocation reactions (DDRs), which can provide a new insight into the topological nature of SDGBM and associated superplastic behaviors. As indicated in pioneer works on topological defects, one important type of crystalline defect, disclination, should be taken into account to understand superplastic deformation mechanisms (Kleman and Friedel, 2008; Mermin, 1979). In terms of symmetry, dislocations originate from the breaking of continuous translational symmetry in discrete lattices, the characteristics of which are quantified by the so-called Burgers vector (Zhang and Ngan, 2018). In parallel, disclinations originate from the breaking of continuous rotational symmetry, the characteristics of which are quantified by the so-called Frank vector (deWit, 1973). Recent studies on dis­ clinations also suggested the potential application of disclination theory on GB migration at elevated temperatures (Hirth et al., 2020; Ovid’Ko and Sheinerman, 2017). By utilizing the disclination theory framework (Cleja-Ţigoiu et al., 2019, 2016; Kirchheim, 2019; Sun et al., 2018, 2016), we have developed a DDR-based superplasticity model to investigate the intrinsic correlation between GB migration and dislocation motion, which suggests a fundamental relationship between microstructural evolution and superplastic deformation. Disclinations have been widely reported in the literature, as observed in grain interior (Motylenko et al., 2004), the twin boundaries and triple/quadruple junctions (Romanov and Kolesnikova, 2009). At the continuum level, based on Nye’s curvature tensor (Nye, 1953) and deWit’s theory (deWit, 1973), the elastic curvature tensor is defined as the gradient of elastic rotation vector, and the disclination density tensor can be determined as the curl of the elastic curvature tensor, which provides a theoretical foundation to determine disclinations by using Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) and Electron Backscatter Diffraction (EBSD) data. Beasuir and Fressengeas proposed a numerical method for calculating the components of the disclination density tensor using EBSD orientation mapping (Beausir and Fressengeas, 2013). Here we adopt this method to investigate the evolution of disclinations during the su­ perplastic deformation. In this work, we designed a new low-alloyed ZXKQ1000 with a stabilized fine-grained structure (grain size ~4.3 μm) enhanced by solute segregation, achieving ~450% superplastic strain. Through quasi-in-situ EBSD analyses, the superplastic behaviors under different strains were systematically studied. It has been found that intragranular dislocations can be absorbed by migrating GBs, which significantly reduces stored elastic energy, without producing stress concentration at GBs (as suggested by a low disclination density at GBs). According to the disclination theory, we report a DDR-based superplastic deformation mechanism that agrees with experimental observations. Our new results suggest a promising design strategy of low-alloyed superplastic Mg systems and shed light on understanding the correlation between DDR-mediated SDGBM mechanism and superplastic processing. Experimental procedure The nominal composition of ZXKQ1000 alloy is Mg-1Zn-0.2Ca-0.2Zr-0.1Ag (wt.%), and its chemical composition is Mg-0.96Zn0.18Ca-0.14Zr-0.11Ag (wt.%), which was measured using an optical spectrum analyzer (ARL 4460, Switzerland). The magnesium alloy ZXKQ1000 was designed to utilize GB segregation of Zn, Ca, Zr and Ag elements to enhance superplasticity in low-alloyed Mg systems. Besides the GB segregation of elements (Nie et al., 2008; Pan et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2020), the co-addition of Zn and Ca elements can weaken the basal texture of Mg alloys (Zeng et al., 2016), Zr element can significantly refine the cast microstructure, which is beneficial to obtain fine-grained structures (Balasubramani et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2014). The Ag segregation can also enhance the thermal stability of microstructure (Miao et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2020). The commercially pure Mg (99.90 wt.%) was firstly melted in an electric furnace under a protective gas mixture of 99.5% CO2 and 0.5% SF6 at ~953 K. After the melting of pure Mg, the pure Zn (99.90 wt.%), Mg-25Ca (wt.%), Mg-30Zr (wt.%) and pure Ag (99.90 wt. %) were put into pure Mg melt. The designed burning rate of Zn, Ca, Zr and Ag is 0 wt.%, 20 wt.%, 10 wt.% and 20 wt.%, respectively. After stirring and removing slags, the melt was directly casted as thin slabs with a thickness of ~6 mm. The as-cast ZXKQ1000 ingot was directly rolled from 5 mm to 1.3 mm by 13 passes. The roller temperature was 100 ◦ C and the sample was pre-heated at 275–300 ◦ C for 10 min before ~10% reduction per pass. The diameter of roller is 150 mm, and its speed is 20 rpm. Thereafter, the hot-rolled sheets were annealed at 300 ◦ C for 5 min. Tensile specimens with a gauge dimension of 10 mm × 4 mm × 1.15 mm were machined from the annealed sheets along the rolling direction (RD). The tensile tests were conducted at 200–300 ◦ C under initial strain rates ranging from 1 ×10− 4 s− 1 to 1 ×10− 2 s− 1 by using an AGS-10 KN machine. The specimens were stabilized at experiment temperature for 5 min before tensile tests. Precise temperature control was guaranteed by the high temperature test chamber made by Jilin Sandu testing machine limited company, whose temperature deviation at 200–300 ◦ C is ± 2.5 ◦ C. According to ASTM standard (E2448), the strain rate change (SRC) tests were carried out at 300 ◦ C in the strain-rate ranges between 1 ×10− 3 s− 1 and 1.2 × 10− 3 s− 1 . A pre-strain of ε = 0.15 was imposed at an initial strain rate of 1 ×10− 3 s− 1 before the SRC test was applied to stabilize the microstructure. The applied strain (between the change of strain rate) was 0.1 after the pre-straining. The details of microstructural evolution were determined by using a SEM (ZEISS Sigma 500) equipped with an EBSD (Oxford Instrument) at the operating voltage of 15 kV, and a transmission electron microscope (TEM, JEM 2100F) at the operating voltage of 200 kV. The EBSD scan step size for all examples was 0.3 μm which can meet the resolution of fine grain microstructure (average grain size ~4.3 μm). The data files of EBSD scans were processed by using the Channel 5.0 data analysis software. In preparation for EBSD characterizations, the samples were ground and polished by using 50 nm SiO2 suspension, and electro-polishing was conducted on polished samples in chilled AC2 electrolyte at 20 V for 90 s. For TEM characterizations, thin foil specimens were prepared by me­ chanical grinding to ~55 μm thickness and ion-beam thinning utilizing a Gatan 691 Precision Ion Polishing System. 3. Results 3.1. Quasi-in-situ EBSD characterizations during superplastic deformation To further understand the deformation mechanisms of the superplastic ZXKQ1000 alloy, quasi-in-situ EBSD was performed to track the micro-textural and microstructural evolutions for the annealed specimen with the tensile strain of 0%, ~10% and ~30% at 300 ◦ C under an initial strain rate of 1 × 10− 3 s − 1. A pre-heating for 5 min was imposed before each tension to stabilize the microstructure. The specimen was polished before pre-heating and tension. The acquisition area of EBSD is in the middle of the gauge. A fraction of the specimen’s surface cannot be indexed by EBSD, due to the surface dimple in those GBs or the high strain in those regions. These unindexed regions are shown by dark color in the EBSD orientation maps. The grain size and grain shape are almost unchanged during the deformation from 0% to ~30% strain (Fig. 4(a-c)). The micro-textural evolution of ZXKQ1000 is shown in Fig. 4(a-c), with 0%, ~10% and ~30% tensile strain, respectively. The peak intensity of 0%, ~10% and ~30% strained specimens is 6.01, 5.44 and 5.98 mrd, respectively. The basal texture tends to be enhanced with the tensile strain of 0%, ~10% and ~30%. Quasi-in-situ EBSD was also performed to track the micro-textural and microstructural evolution for the annealed specimen at a later deformation stage, with ~100%, ~120% and ~140% tensile strain. The EBSD data were collected from the polished tensile specimen after testing to ~100% strain at 300 ◦ C under an initial strain rate 1 × 10− 3 s − 1. There is no significant change in grain size and shape during the deformation from ~100% to ~140% strain (Fig. 4(d-f)). The micro-textural evolution of ZXKQ1000 subjected to ~100%, ~120% and ~140% strain is shown in Fig. 4(d-f). The texture intensities of the specimen with ~100%, ~120% and ~140% tensile strain are 5.40 mrd, 5.22 mrd and 5.25 mrd, respectively, and they have similar texture distributions (Fig. 4(d-f)). In contrast to the gradually increased basal texture of the specimen with ~0%, ~10% and ~30% tensile strain, the texture of specimen with ~100%, ~120% and ~140% tensile strain remains almost unchanged. Discussion By using solute segregation to enhance the stability of the fine-grained structure, we have shown a new low-alloyed ZXKQ1000 with ~450% superplastic strain in the previous section. To further understand the relationship between microstructure evolution and superplastic properties, as well as the deformation mechanism in the low-alloyed ZXKQ1000 alloy, additional experimental and theoretical analyses were performed, including (i) the determination of strain rate sensitivity, slip systems and GBS contribution, (ii) measurement of dislocation and disclination densities and (iii) establishment of DDR model for GB migration mechanism. 4.1. The determination of strain rate sensitivity, slip systems and GBS contribution As suggested in the literature (Padmanabhan and Prabu, 2018), strain rate sensitivity (m) is an important superplastic indicator for deformation at elevated temperatures, which is associated with the deformation mechanism related to diffusional/diffusionless processes. m can be determined through the slope of the stress-strain rate curve, as defined in σ = kε̇m . According to the ASTM standard (E2448), SRC tests were carried out at 300 ◦ C in the strain-rate ranges between 1 ×10− 3 s− 1 and 1.2 ×10− 3 s− 1 (Fig. 5). The flow stress (in strain of 0.1) is ~52 MPa, which is approximately equal to the flow stress (~50 MPa) in Fig. 3(c) and Fig. 3(d) within the standard deviation. In general, the characteristic m values are 0.5 and 0.2 for GBS and dislocation slip (or creep) during superplastic deformation, respectively (Kim et al., 2017, 2008; Panicker et al., 2009). In our samples, the value of m, between ~0.39 and ~0.42, suggests a cooperative mechanism of GB migration and dislocation slip, with GB migration making significant contributions at this 7 International Journal of Plasticity 154 (2022) 103300 C. According to the literature (Li and Qi, 2013; Wu et al., 2014), the enhancement of basal texture during the deformation is due to the occurrence of basal slip, which is consistent with our results. The distribution characteristics of in-grain misorientation axes (IGMA) were investigated to determine the occurrence of slip mode during the superplastic deformation. This method has been widely used to determine the active slip modes of HCP materials, for example Mg (Chun et al., 2010; Hadorn et al., 2012). The theory of the method is based on the hypothesis that the action of slip leads to lattice bending taken place about an axis (termed as Taylor axis). Therefore, it is possible to determine the dominant slip mode in a deformed crystal by matching the misorientation axis. The Taylor axes for active slip modes in Mg alloys are displayed in Table 3. Due to the limited angular resolution of the EBSD analysis, the minimum misorientation angle should be determined beforehand. By measuring the intragranular misorientation of full recrystallized grains, we found that 95% of misorientation angles are less than 0.8◦ , which was regarded as the minimum misorientation angle. The maximum misorientation angle of the general GB is assigned as 3◦ . Thus, only the misorientation axes of the material-point pairs with misorientation angle between 0.8◦ and 3◦ are plotted in the IGMA distribution. Fig. 7(a) shows the typical IPF map of the ~10% strained sample. The IGMA distribution of the selected grains (marked as 1 through 28 in Fig. 7(a)) are presented in Fig. 7(b). It found that only 3 deformed grains (number: 9, 12 and 13) exhibit a characteristic <0001> Taylor axis, and most of the other grains exhibit a trend concentrated towards the <uvt0> axis. Grain boundary migration through disclination-dislocation reactions (DDR) Based on our measurements of strain rate sensitivity, strain contribution of GBS, IGMA analyses, dislocation density and dis­ clination density in Sections 4.1 and 4.2, cooperative deformation mechanisms of dislocation slip and GB migration during the su­ perplastic deformation of the ZXKQ1000 alloy are suggested. However, the contributions of GB migration and intragranular slip are different during the deformation. Intragranular dislocation slip dominates the initial deformation (suggested by IGMA analyses, textural evolution and the increase of GND density), meanwhile GB migration also plays an important role in coordinating. After the GND density reached the peak, GB migration makes significant contributions during the next stage (suggested by our measured strain rate sensitivity m between ~0.39 and ~0.42, and strain contribution of GBS εGBS ≈ 63%). In particular, GB migration is achieved by consuming nearby intragranular dislocations, as implied by the decrease of intragranular dislocation density, which is driven by both external and internal stress. Disclination theory originates from the topological theory of defects, in which dislocation and disclination are two basic types of topological defects in crystals (Mermin, 1979). A disclination can be quantified by its Frank vector, which is the lattice rotation vector. Similar to the classification of dislocations, disclinations can be classified into wedge disclinations (Frank vector is parallel to dis­ clination line) and twist disclinations (Frank vector is perpendicular to disclination line) (Volterra, 1907). An individual disclination is difficult to be present in solid crystals, because of severe local elastic distortion (Hirth et al., 2020). However, self-screened config­ urations (such as disclination dipole and disclination loop) can exist. Disclination loops in a crystalline solid can also exist as the limits of boundaries (Kleman and Friedel, 2008). The structure of a given GB can be regarded as a disclination loop and a set of attached dislocations, which bear the relative displacement along the boundary. Based on such a description, GB migration can be considered as the result of disclination/dislocation motions (Romanov and Kolesnikova, 2009). When a disclination moves, associated dislocations will be either emitted or absorbed (Kleman and Friedel, 2008). The relation between the Frank vector of disclinations and the Burgers vector of dislocations can be investigated through their topological reaction, which is fundamentally similar to the dislocation reaction in classical dislocation theory (Kleman and Friedel, 2008). Note that the motion of dislocations is associated with GB migration in the above model. Before the merge of triple junctions I and II, GB migration is driven by absorbing intragranular dislocations, which significantly reduces stored elastic energy, with a decrease of intragranular dislocation density (suggested by the decrease of GND density during the strain from ~20% to ~100% and the GBs migration absorbing dislocations in Fig. 10(c) and (f)). When intragranular dislocation density reaches a relatively low level, a dy­ namic equilibrium can be established with the dislocations produced by intragranular slip (suggested by the GND density during the strain from ~100% to ~450% and GND variation in Fig. 11(c) and (f)) and dislocations absorbed by GB migrations. The split of triple junctions III and IV will be achieved through emitting dislocations with opposite sign in our model. Emitted dislocations can be selfannihilated along GBs, which does not change intragranular dislocation density. The self-annihilation of GB dislocations can directly lead to experimentally-observed sliding offset at GBs. In fact, both dislocation absorption and emission can occur simultaneously in reality, the relative contributions of which depend on the type and density of intragranular dislocations. Note that the GB migration model can be utilized to analyze the absorption and emission of dislocations by other GB-related processes at elevated temperatures (e. g., the formation of sub-grain boundary through dislocation rearrangement, grain rotation), which can also result in the decrease of intragranular dislocation density during superplastic deformation. In the current DDR model, continuous dynamic recrystallization can be captured, while discontinuous dynamic recrystallization, e.g., nucleation of new grains, has not considered, since no apparent nucleation phenomenon has been observed in our experiments. In general, the DDR-based GB migration model is a new theoretical description of the coevolution for GB and dislocation/disclination. Depending on the types of dislocations/disclinations, DDR requires both diffusionless and diffusional motions of disclinations and dislocations, which may only happen at elevated temperatures. On the one hand, disclinations are usually distributed at GB triple junctions, which can move with GB migration and require short range diffusion. On the other hand, the reactions between disclinations and dislocations need specific types of dislocations moving towards GB triple junction, which requires both glide and climb of dislocations. At the atomic scale, GB migration and dislocation climb require either short-range or long-range diffusion of atoms, which happen at high temperatures. By employing disclination theory, we have quantified the intrinsic correlation between GB migration and dislocation motions through DDR. Our result is also consistent with a number of phenomenological models in the literature. Ashby and Verrall (1973) constructed the model of GB migration around triple junctions, which keeps equiaxed grain structures during superplasticity. Cordier et al. (2014) suggested that disclination motion was related to the migration of GBs, leading to macroscopic deformation of olivine-rich rocks. Kirchheim (2019) also suggested that triple junctions could move by the creation or annihilation of disclination dipoles. Our GB migration model based on DDR elucidated the mechanisms involved in the superplastic deformation, which can substantially advance our understanding of microstructure evolutions (e.g., GB, dislocation, disclination) and superplastic properties. 5. Conclusions In this work, we designed a new low-alloyed ZXKQ1000 superplastic Mg system and investigated the relationship between its microstructure and superplastic deformation behaviors. Our major findings include: (1) By utilizing co-segregation of Zn, Ca, Zr and Ag atoms along the GBs, we obtained a ZXKQ1000 alloy with a stabilized fine-grain structure (grain size ~4.3 μm), which can exhibit a superplastic elongation of ~450% at 300 ◦ C under an initial strain rate of 1 × 10− 3 s − 1. 16 International Journal of Plasticity 154 (2022) 103300 C. Du et al. (2) According to the results of the strain rate change (SRC) tests and dislocation/disclination density analyses, we found that GB migration could be driven by absorbing intragranular dislocations, without producing stress concentration at GBs (as suggested by a low disclination density at GBs). (3) Our dislocation/disclination density analyses suggest a new GB migration model for superplastic deformation mechanism. The Burgers vectors of dislocations absorbed/emitted by GBs can be quantitatively determined during GB migration. This model also suggests a possible mechanism to achieve sliding offset, i.e. dislocations with opposite sign are self-annihilated along GBs. (4) The GB migration model based on DDR can be used to understand the relationship between microstructure and superplasticity. The superplastic deformation behavior of the ZXKQ1000 alloy originates from cooperative deformation mechanisms of dislocation slip and GB migration. These results not only suggest a promising way to design low-alloyed superplastic Mg systems, but also shed light on understanding the intrinsic correlation between GB migration and dislocation motion at elevated temperatures, which establishes a fundamental relationship between the evolution of microstructural defects and the strain accommodation of superplastic deformation.
