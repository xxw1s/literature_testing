In this paper, WC particles (WCp) reinforced Fe-based metal matrix composites (WCp/Fe) were manufactured by laser melting deposition (LMD) technology to investigate the characteristics of cracks formation. The section morphology of composites were analyzed by optical microscope (OM), and microstructure of WCp, matrix and interface were analyzed by X-ray diffraction (XRD) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), in order to study the crack initiation and propagation behavior under different laser process conditions. The temperature of materials during the laser melting deposition was detected by the infrared thermometer. The results showed that the cracks often appeared after ﬁve layers laser deposition in this experiment. The cracks crossed through WC particles rather than the interface, so the strength of interface obtained by the LMD was relatively large. When the thermal stress induced by high temperature gradient during LMD and the coefﬁcient of thermal expansion mismatch between WC and matrix was larger than yield strength of WC, the cracks would initiate inside WC particle. Cracks mostly propagated along the eutectic phases whose brittleness was very large. The obtained thin interface was beneﬁcial to transmitting the stress from particle to matrix. The inﬂuence of volume fraction of particles, laser power and scanning speed on cracks were investigated. This paper investigated the inﬂuence of WC particles size on cracks systematically, and the smallest size of cracked WC in different laser processing parameters was also researched. & 2016 Elsevier Ltd. Introduction In recent years, metal matrix composites (MMC) have been widely investigated by researchers due to their excellent mechanical and thermal properties [1]. To increase the wear resistance and corrosion of materials at room or high temperature, WC particles with favorable properties such as high melting point, high hardness and a good wettability with molten bonding metals, which are widely used as the reinforcement of Fe-based alloy to form the ceramic reinforced Fe-based metal matrix composites during laser melting deposition (LMD) [2,3]. With the rapid development of laser additive manufacturing technology, the fabrication of MMC parts by LMD has been becoming possible in industrial revolution today. The processes of LMD are based on layerby-layer materials melting and deposition process to build complete parts. LMD provides a new technology to fabricate complex components of difﬁcult-to-process metal matrix composites [4]. n Corresponding author. E-mail address: wangjd251@126.com (J. An apparent advantage of manufacturing MMC by LMD is that it can realize the free design of reinforcement volume fractions and manufacture gradient MMC [5]. It is generally believed that cracks in MMC parts manufacturing are crucial to the reliable material properties, especially for the reinforcement particles with high volume fraction. Some works about the crack characteristics have been done during the past several years. P.B. Kadolkar [6] stated that the formation of cracks during laser cladding was related to the thermophysical properties of depositing materials and substrate, such as melting point, coefﬁcient of thermal expansion and Young's modulus. Shengfeng Zhou [7] demonstrated that cracks depended on the microstructure of Ni-based WC composites and laser processing parameters such as laser power and scan speed. In Changmin Lee's work [8], the residual stress distributions of the Co-based WC þNiCr composites were measured, and he believed that residual stress could be potential driving force to provide an easy crack path ways for large brittle fractures combined with the crack initiation sites such as the fractured WC particles, pores and solidiﬁcation cracks. Subrata Kumar Ghosh [10] researched crack density and wear performance of SiC particulate (SiCp) reinforced Al-based metal matrix composite (Al-MMC) fabricated by direct metal laser sintering (DMLS) process, he found that crack density increased signiﬁcantly after 15 vol percentage (vol%) of SiCp, because with the amount of reinforcement increasing, the chances of the clustering effect that resisted the ﬂow of molten material increased, which led to the formation of pores in the specimens. He also clariﬁed that the relationship between the size of reinforcement and wear resistance of the composite, but he did not illuminated the inﬂuence of reinforced particles size on cracks. Liu [11] studied the fracture behavior of metal matrix composites reinforced with monocrystalline WC particles. He found that there were two crack nucleation mechanisms in the WC particles. These previous researches are very useful for the better understanding of the cracks formation. As is known to all, the materials are easy to suffer from high residual stress during the process of LMD, which will lead to the formation of cracks in the materials. The cracks generation during the LMD remains an unsolved problem [8]. Under the action of applied load, the cracks will result in premature failure and consequently rather low performance in the metal matrix composites manufactured by LMD. So it is necessary to investigate the crack characteristics in the MMC produced by LMD. In this paper, WC particles (WCp) reinforced Fe-based metal matrix composites (WCp/Fe) are manufactured by LMD technology. The cracks formation, which include crack initiation and propagation, and the inﬂuences of WC size and volume fraction and laser processing parameters on the cracks have been systematically investigated. 2. Experiments The materials used in this work were WC powders and 316 L stainless steel powders. As we know, the particles with irregular shape were easy to generate stress concentration in the sharp corners [12], so the spherical powders were selected. Morphology and cracks of WCp/Fe The deposited MMC presented a geometrical size of approximately 43 mm  3.4 mm  2 mm when the laser power was 300 W, laser scanning speed was 0.3 m/min, WC volume fraction was 28.6%. The WC particles were distributed randomly in the matrix, as can be seen from the morphology of the MMC in Fig. 4 (a), and there were little pores in the materials. However, the cracks often appeared after ﬁve layers laser deposition under the above laser processing condition from the Fig. 4(b). The microstructures of WC particles, matrix and the interfaces were mainly concerned in this paper. As shown in Fig. 9(a), the matrix was composed of dendrite structures and interdendritic eutectic phases. The thickness of interface was very thin ranging from 0.9 to 1.8 mm (Fig.9(b)). Liqun Li [15] reported that this interface obtained by laser melt injection was formed only at rapid cooling rates. He researched the WCp/Ti interface zone prepared by a vacuum arc furnace melting process. It was found that under condition of slow cooling rates, the thickness of interface was nearly 10 mm, which was largely thicker than that produces by laser melt injection. Laser melting deposition and laser melt injection all utilized the laser as the heat source, so the interface gained by LMD was very thin. The thin interface was beneﬁcial to transmitting the stress from matrix to particle. The X-ray diffraction results of WCP/Fe composites exhibited in Fig.10 demonstrated that the composites were composed of WC, W2C, γFe, Fe3C, Fe2W, M6C, M7C3, M23C6(M ¼Fe, W, Cr). WC particles were dissolved partly to form W and C atoms during laser melting deposition [3], some of which reacted with Fe-based alloy to precipitate M6C, M7C3, M23C6 carbides during the rapid solidiﬁcation. As a result, the matrix consisted of dendrite (γFe) and (γFeþ carbides) eutectic phase. 3.4. Cracks initiation 3.2. The thermal stress was induced by high temperature gradient during LMD and the different coefﬁcient of thermal expansion between WC and matrix [7]. The residual stress might not be sufﬁcient enough for generating the cracks at the 316 L stainless steel due to the high toughness and ductility of the Fe-based alloy [8]. However, during the rapid heating and cooling process of LMD, high temperature gradient would lead to the generation of thermal stress in WC particle because the coefﬁcient of thermal expansion of WC particle was lower than that of 316 L stainless steel. When the thermal stress was larger than yield strength of WC, the micro-cracks formed and propagated in the weaker place of WC particles, then penetrated through WC particles ﬁnally. The microstructure of WC particle was shown in Fig. 12. The WC particles used in this experiment were polycrystalline. There were many grain boundaries in the surface of WC (Fig. 12(a)). The microstructure inside WC consisted of (WC þW2C) eutectic phases, as shown in Fig. 12(b). The grain boundaries were formed between the (WC þW2C) eutectic clusters. It was generally believed that the grain boundaries were weaker place where the cracks were easy to form and propagate. Fig. 14 presents the micrographs of composites containing WC particles with different size. The WC particles size was closely related to the cracks in MMC. The micrographs revealed that the cracks were easier to generate in the WC particles more than 30 mm when laser power was 300 W, scanning speed was 0.6 m/ min and WC volume fraction was 16.7%. Compared to the WC particles with small size, those with large size were more prone to have defects [4,17]. In addition, the larger WC particles size, the larger interfaces between Fe matrix and WC particles, and the larger stress transmitting from interfaces to particles. Furthermore, the WC particles with large size were hard to ﬂow together with matrix in the process of composites plastic deformation. Therefore, they were easy to generate stress concentration. The WC particles tended to crack more easily where they distributed concentratively because of high stress concentration. The crack of particles usually attributed to largest principal stress, which increased with the size of particles rising [18,19]. As a result, the WC particles with small size whose largest principal stress was small were hard to crack. 3.7. The inﬂuence of scanning speed on cracks Scanning speed was another factor that affected the formation of cracks. The cracks formed easily when scanning speed increased from 0.3 m/min to 0.6 m/min (Fig. 25 and Fig. 26). Because when scanning speed increased, the cooling rate in the laser heated area increased more quickly, which would induce larger stress. The very high heating and cooling rates during the LMD process were the main sources for crack initiation as well as residual stress evolution [20,21]. Meanwhile, increasing scanning speed increased the thermal shock. However, unlike the result of different laser power, the smallest sizes of cracked WC particles had no apparent change when scanning speed increased from 0.3 m/min to 0.6 m/min, and they were all nearly 31 mm (Fig. 27 and Fig. 28). 4.
