Its driving mechanisms are multiple that can generally be classified as concentration gradient,1 pH gradient,2 surfactantinduced flow,3 and so on. Among these origins, temperature gradient, especially, has received significant attention and research. To most liquids, there is a negative relationship between surface tension and temperature, namely dg/dT o 0, indicating that the flow direction on the fluid surface is from the hot side to the cold side,4–6 or a droplet moving in the direction along the decreasing temperature on a plate.7,8 As a special fluid, the Marangoni flow of liquid metal has recently attracted extensive interest. Previous studies were mainly focused on liquid metals with high melting points. For example, the gas bubbles movement in molten steel induced by the Marangoni eﬀect was found to be critical to improve the quality of casting.9 Moreover, the Marangoni flow of welded points was studied to understand the heat transfer and flow mechanism.10,11 In recent years, the research of Marangoni flow on room temperature liquid metal, usually gallium or its alloys, a Beijing Key Lab of CryoBiomedical Engeering and Key Lab of Cryogenics, Technical Institute of Physics and Chemistry, Chinese Academy of Sciences, Beijing 100190, China. As distinct from liquid metals with a high melting point, room temperature liquid metals were easily induced to generate Marangoni flow in an aqueous solution with an external electric field.12,13 The mechanism lies in the fact that the interface between a gallium-based alloy and the aqueous solution could be formed with an electrical double layer (EDL), which can be easily regulated by an electric field. It is this modified EDL that has led to the surface tension diﬀerence, which induced a Marangoni flow on a liquid metal surface and actuation of the objects. Moreover, an oxygen reduction reaction can also result in a surface tension gradient on a liquid metal surface and subsequently induce the Marangoni flow and self-actuation.14,15 In this study, we disclosed a new Marangoni flow phenomenon across the interface between liquid gallium and acid solution caused by the Cu–Ga galvanic corrosion couple. When the semiconductor cooling plate placed above the copper bulk properly worked, gallium at the section near the copper bulk started to solidify. At the other end, gallium was heated to a high temperature via the reverse side of another semiconductor plate. When the temperature field reached an equilibrium state, a rapid flow of the HCl solution overlaying the liquid gallium can be observed (more details can be found in the Video S1, ESI†), whose direction runs from the cold side to the hot side at the region near copper. Because the surface of liquid gallium was very smooth, like a mirror, the flow of liquid metal could not be directly observed. The surface current of the HCl solution could be known by analyzing the motion of bubbles, which was generated by the reaction between HCl and liquid gallium. Fig. 1(b) demonstrates a global image of the superficial solution streamline. The flow on liquid gallium surface was distributed in two regions: the forward flow region attached to copper and the counter flow region at the far end. Mechanism of liquid metal Marangoni flow With the elimination of the Rayleigh–Bénard convection influence, we analyzed the contribution of another factor: the surface tension gradient caused by the temperature drop. In general, the relationship between surface tension and temperature was linear and negative: dg/dT o 0. This surface tension gradient would drive the flow at the interface towards the higher surface tension region (lower temperature region), which was contrary to the experimental result. Therefore, a supplementary experiment was conducted for further exploration. We removed the copper bulk and transferred the semiconductor cooling plate to the bottom. No obvious Marangoni flow could be observed when the temperature field of liquid gallium became stabilized. This indicated that the surface gradient, which was directly generated by the temperature difference (below 20 1C) was too small to drive the liquid gallium surface flow. After further analysis, the reason of the Marangoni flow was attributed to the kinetic behavior caused by electrochemical reaction. When the metal gallium alone was immersed in HCl solution, metal oxidation occurred through an anodic reaction and hydrogen reduction occurred through a cathodic reaction as shown below: Consequently, both anodic and cathodic reactions are coupled in a corrosion process. The potential of the isolated gallium electrode (Ec), which was also called the corrosion potential, could be acquired through mixed potential diagrams.16 The corrosion potential Ec was higher than Ee(Ga/Ga3+), but lower than Ee(H/H+), where Ee(Ga/Ga3+) and Ee(H/H+) were the equilibrium potential of anodic and cathodic reactions, respectively. Then, engaging copper into this interaction, a copper– gallium corrosion couple would form. Thus, the essence of this phenomenon was a galvanic corrosion problem, which was similar to some common corrosion couples such as the steel– carbon couple, copper–zinc couple, and titanium–zinc couple. In this copper–gallium corrosion couple, due to the higher equilibrium potential of copper (Ee(Cu/Cu2+)) relative to Ee(H/H+), copper would not dissolve and act as a cathode for reaction (b). There would be current flow from copper to gallium. For the gallium electrode, reactions (a) and (b) simultaneously occurred. However, there is only reduction reaction (b) on the copper electrode. Therefore, in terms of the dissolution of gallium, it was mainly caused and accelerated by the galvanic corrosion couple, which was formed by contact with the copper, besides the reason of the coupled reactions (a) and (b). As for the potential distribution on the corrosion couple, previous literature17,18 has given the theoretical and experimental results. The inserted small figure of Fig. 4(b) showed the schematic of the electrode potential distribution in solution across the surface of a galvanic corrosion couple. The potential distribution was between Ecorr-A and Ecorr-B and varied with the distance from the A–B junction. Ecorr-A and Ecorr-B were the free corrosion potential of element A and B in the solution, respectively. To obtain quantitative data herein, we measured the potential distribution of the Cu–Ga corrosion couple on gallium surface. The experimental arrangement is schematically shown in Fig. 4(a). The positive pole of the electrometer was connected with the copper bulk and the negative pole was connected with a saturated calomel reference electrode (SCE). The measurement result indicated that the potential distribution was very regular. The potential reached a maximum value at the position of the Cu–Ga junction and then decreased with the increase of the distance from this junction. There was a pattern that the minimum point of the potential corresponded to the transition point of Marangoni flow. In the range of Cu–Ga junction to transition point, the potential drops and the Marangoni flow goes forward along the x axis. In the Video S2 (ESI†), we can also find that the Marangoni flow in the forward flow region was still faster than that in the counter flow region due to a higher gradient of potential distribution rather. 4 Conclusions In summary, the Marangoni flow of liquid gallium in an acidic environment was the centre of our attention. Liquid gallium in the channel would have obvious Marangoni flow when it was contacted with copper bulk and formed a Cu–Ga corrosion couple. The interior and exterior flow field distributions of liquid gallium were quantitatively studied to explore the mechanism of the intense flow phenomenon. First, natural convection and temperature gradient were excluded as intrinsic reasons via numerical simulation and control experiments. Then, the main cause was attributed to the kinetic behavior caused by the galvanic corrosion couple, which led to the nonuniform potential distribution and even the formation of a surface tension gradient on liquid gallium’s surface. Moreover, this conclusion was further confirmed by combining the experimental measurement of liquid gallium surface potential and the thermocapillary effect. Finally, the variation of the Marangoni flow under different temperature conditions was studied.
