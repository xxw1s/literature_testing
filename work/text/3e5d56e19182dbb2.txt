The reaction of WC in the molten pool releases a large amount of thermal energy, which gives the molten pool a faster flow rate. Introduction Laser cladding technology can effectively improve the wear resis­ tance of metals; however, the wear resistance of the coating is limited by the pure alloy powder used [1,2]. Ceramic particles can be added to the alloy powder to improve the coating’s wear resistance. There has been much research on the wear resistance of composite coatings containing ceramic particles such as B4C [3], SiC [4,5], TiC [6] and WC [7,8]. Ceramic particles can effectively alleviate wear damage to the coating, thus improving its wear resistance. To enhance wear resistance, composite coatings reinforced with insitu TiC and (Ti, Nb)C particles have been investigated. Dispersive NbC particles (average diameter = 1.03 μm) formed in-situ at the grain boundary can provide dispersion and fine-grained strengthening effects. This provides more than five times less volume loss in the composite coating compared to a substrate with Fe-based coating. Alternating formation of TiC and NbC promotes in-situ generation of multiphase (Ti, Nb)C and prevents NbC from agglomerating. The wear resistance of a Ni204 composite coating was improved by around 11 times [9,10]. To reveal the effects of the content and size of in-situ synthesized TiC on the mechanical properties of matrix composites, a series of Ni-based composite coatings was fabricated by direct energy deposition. The wear mechanism changed from adhesive wear to abrasive wear and finally to fatigue wear. As the content of in-situ synthesized TiC particles in­ creases, the hardness and wear-resistance of the coatings increase [11,12]. Multi-layer cladding is an effective way to conduct high-thickness additive manufacturing over large areas. Because multi-layer laser cladding causes a large amount of heat accumulate in the coating, it produces high thermal stress within it. At the same time, the coating is greatly deformed with increases in thickness. This deformation signifi­ cantly increases the internal residual stress, which increases the proba­ bility of coating crack initiation. Wang Jiandong prepared TiC/Ti6Al4V composites on the surface of Ti6Al4V by multilayer laser deposition. The composites produced cracks that extended from the interface to the top [13]. To alleviate cracking in multilayer cladding, a new class of ma­ terials has been adopted: functionally gradient materials (FGMs). These are novel composites composed of two or more materials and with a continuous gradient in composition and microstructure. These FGMs can significantly improve the binding strength of two materials, eliminating the stress singularity at the intersection point and the stress-free point at the connection interface. The influence of ceramic particles on the microstructure and wear resistance of laser cladding coatings have been well studied. However, WC can dissolve in the molten pool and react with metal and nonmetal elements. The chemical reaction will cause a change in energy that alters the temperature and flow state of the molten pool. The microstructure and mechanical properties of the coating are affected by the trans­ formation in the molten pool. There has been no in-depth study on the effect of WC dissolution on the molten pool and microstructure of coatings. In addition, the impact-resistance of the coatings have received little study. The effect of WC on the impact-resistance of coatings is unknown. Therefore, there is a need to understand the effect of carbide particles on the impact-resistance of coatings. In this paper, a WC-reinforced Ni-based coating was manufactured on 42CrMoA by laser cladding. We reveal the effects of WC on the microstructure, molten pool, wear behaviour and impact-resistance of the coating. This heat dispersion decreases the temperature gradient of the pool and increases its cooling rate. The trend in epitaxial growth is alleviated by a decrease in the temperature gradient and increase in cooling rate. At a WC particle dosage of 12 wt%, the viscosity μ of the molten pool increases. It can be seen from Eqs. (10–12) that Re and FD decrease and τp increases with increases in vis­ cosity μ. The above process reduces the velocity and heat conductivity of WC, thus weakening the inhibition of WC on epitaxial growth. In addition, the flowing WC particles break the cellular crystals at the interface during solidification in the molten pool, as shown in Fig. 24a. The broken crystals gradually form cellular crystals, as shown in Fig. 24b. Therefore, the interfacial microstructure changes from epitaxial columnar crystals to cellular crystals with increases in WC. The equiaxed crystals are gradually formed in the top zone of the coating. In summary, dissolution at the edges of WC particles occurs in the molten pool. The heat released from the reaction promotes flow, which exerts a force on the WC particles so that they move with the melt. The flow of WC particles decreases the temperature gradient and increases the cooling rate. This is because WC particles are brittle and there are a large number of dislocations in them. The dislocation source is far away from the dislocation accumu­ lation front. WC particles produce great stress that leads to the rapid generation of cracks and instantaneous propagation throughout the WC particles. Finally, the cracks lead to cleavage fracture [32,33].
