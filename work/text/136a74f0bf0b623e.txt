The mathematical models between laser processing parameters (laser power, powder feeding rate, scanning speed) and dilution, porosity, and micro­ hardness were developed by response surface methodology (RSM) and validated by ANOVA based on singlefactor experimental results. The results show that the dilution and porosity of the coating are negatively correlated due to the significant difference in melting point between the Ti6Al4V substrate and the RHEA powder. The rise in the dilution causes a decrease in the content of the strengthening elements in the coating, which is the main reason for the sudden drop in the microhardness to 425 HV. Compared to laser power and powder feeding rate, the analyses of variance show that the scanning speed has a relatively high F-value of 70.38. Also, the dilution has a positive correlation with laser power and scanning speed, and is inversely correlated with the powder feeding rate. Finally, a multi-objective optimization in terms of dilution, porosity, and microhardness was performed, and the optimal combination of processing parameters was a laser power of 2700 W, a powder feeding rate of 8 g/min, and a scanning speed of 3 mm/s. The predicted results are in good agreement with the validation results. 1. Introduction Laser cladding (LC) is a surface modification technology that uses a high-energy laser beam to melt and deposit specific material on the surface of the substrate, so as to improve the surface properties of the substrate [1,2]. LC is widely used in aerospace, petrochemical, and other industries to repair parts and enhance the comprehensive performance of the surface because of its advantages of rapid melting and solidifi­ cation, modest heat-affected zone, and high bonding strength [3–5]. Existing reports indicate that the mechanical properties and service life of the substrate can be effectively improved by controlling the pro­ cessing parameters of laser cladding [6,7]. Titanium alloys are receiving more and more attention because of their high intensity, small density, and good corrosion resistance [8], but the poor wear resistance and high-temperature properties largely limit their development [9], especially in the aerospace field. LC is an effec­ tive method for strengthening the surface of titanium alloys. Huang et al. [10] prepared TiVCrAlSi high-entropy alloy coating with few cracks and pores on Ti6Al4V by LC. The study showed that the hardness and wear resistance of the coating were significantly higher than that of Ti6Al4V due to the generation of intermetallic compounds (Ti, V)5Si3. Zhu et al. [11] significantly improved the surface properties of Ti6Al4V substrate by LC of Co-Cu/Ti3SiC self-lubricating wear resistant composite coat­ ings, which could be attributed to the synergistic effect of the dispersion strengthening, solid solution, and grain refinement strengthening. In addition, the LC of lightweight refractory AlTiVMoNb high-entropy al­ loys on the Ti6Al4V substrates exhibits excellent oxidation resistance. Chen et al. [12] found the average oxidation weight gain of the coating is 4.94 mg⋅cm− 2 after being oxidized at 800 ◦ C for 120 h, which is only 10.58 % of the Ti6Al4V substrate (46.7 mg⋅cm− 2). Recent developments in LC powders and surface coatings have gone beyond traditional ma­ terials [13]. As a new group of alloys, refractory high-entropy alloys (RHEA) composed of high melting point elements, such as W, Mo, Ta, Nb, etc., were first successfully prepared in 2010 by Senkov et al. [14]. The refractory TiZrNbWMo high-entropy alloy synthesized by Zhang et al. [20] possessed high phase stability, high microhardness, and excep­ tional resistance to softening. The microstructure of the high-entropy alloy remained almost after annealing at 1200 ◦ C for 20 h, and its microhardness reached up to 1300 HV0.5 at 800 ◦ C. Li et al. The results showed that its yield strength, compressive strength, and compressive strain at 1000 ◦ C were 530 MPa, 684 MPa, and 8.5 %, respectively, suggesting better high-temperature properties than conventional re­ fractory metals. The preparation of RHEA coating on the surface of titanium alloys by LC is undoubtedly an effective surface modification method. However, the high laser reflectivity of titanium alloys and the extremely high melting points of refractory elements pose greater difficulties for laser processing [22]. It is well known that processing parameters, such as laser power, scanning speed, powder feeding rate, carrier gas flow, etc., significantly affect on geometry and properties of the cladding layer [23]. The coating with controllable microstructure and properties can be obtained by optimizing the combination of parameters [24,25]. Empirical-statistical is an effective method to optimize the processing parameters [26]. Lian et al. [27] investigated the influence of processing parameters on 316L/WC coating by grey relational analysis and orthogonal experimental design. The predicted error rate was only 4.92 %, and the results showed that the WC ratio had the greatest effect on the microhardness and wear volume of the coating. Ma et al. [28] established a model of processing parameters on dilution and residual stress using response surface methodology and obtained the smooth Pareto front by a multi-objective quantum-behaved particle swarm optimization algorithm. The results showed that the optimized coating had the best comprehensive performance of 13.10 % dilution and 183.0 MPa residual stress. For preparing RHEA coating with satisfactory quality on the surface of titanium alloys by LC is extremely challenging, this study aims to find the processing window for laser cladding RHEA coating on the surface of titanium alloys. A new refractory MoNbZrTi high-entropy alloy was prepared on Ti6Al4V substrate by LC. The slag inclusions, which distributed mainly in the bottom region of the coating, are believed to be resulted from some unmelted powder parti­ cles with a high-melting point (Supplementary Materials, Fig. 1). As the laser power increases, the coating depth in­ creases, and the dilution of the coating increases almost linearly (Fig. 4 (a) and (b)). In addition, the porosity of the coating reduces with the increasing laser power, because enough power input leads to sufficient melting of refractory elemental powder and adequate convection of the melt pool. The microhardness curves of the coatings prepared with different laser power are shown in Fig. 4(c) - (f). It can be clearly seen that the microhardness of all the coatings decreases gradually along the depth direction, and the MoNbTiZr RHEA coating has the highest microhard­ ness. It is worth noting that the microhardness curves fluctuate fiercely at low laser power and become stable with increasing laser power. The insets of Fig. 4(c) - (f) demonstrate that the anomalous lower micro­ hardness results from the indentation located on or in the vicinity of the particles. Thus, the fluctuation of the microhardness curves is associated with the porosity of the coating. The average microhardness of the coatings is 563.17 HV, 504.21 HV, 483.22 HV, and 503.16 HV at 2100 W, 2400 W, 2700 W, and 3000 W, respectively, exhibiting an overall decreasing trend. The decrease in microhardness is ascribed to the coarsened microstructure (Supplementary Materials, Fig. 2). The increasing laser power prolongs the duration of the melt pool and gives the dendritic more time to grow up [33]. Besides, the slight rise in microhardness from 2700 W to 3000 W may result from the melting of more reinforced powder particles, which promotes the solution strengthening effect. The effect of powder feeding rate on the morphology of the coatings is shown in Fig. 3(b1) - (b4). The laser power and the scanning speed keep at 2400 W and 7 mm/s, respectively. It can be seen that the deeppenetration phenomenon occurs in MoNbTiZr RHEA coating when the powder feeding rate is 7 g/min (Fig. 3(b1)). With the increase of powder feeding rate, the height and porosity of the coating increase as expected (Fig. 5(a) and (b)). The most direct effect of powder feeding rate change is the powder material that is fed for each unit length, which is defined as PF/SS [34]. The increased powder feeding rate leads to a more concentrated powder stream that attenuates the laser energy by the ef­ fects of absorption and reflection of the laser beam [35]. Thus, less laser energy is absorbed by the Ti6Al4V substrate, and the melt pool tem­ perature decreases. This explains the decrease in the width and dilution of the coating with the increasing powder feeding rate. As shown in Fig. 5(c) - (f), the changing law of microhardness curves of the coating at different powder feeding rates are similar to those of the coating at different laser power. The average microhardness of the coatings for different processing parameters is calculated and plotted in Fig. 7(a). It can be seen that the microhardness of the coating rises with the increase in powder feeding rate. It is noteworthy that the coating exhibits a deep-penetration phenomenon when the laser power is 3000 W (Fig. 3(a4)). This phe­ nomenon is related to the change in melt pool temperature caused by the increase of laser power, which leads to the transition of Marangoni stress [30,31]. The temperature coefficient of surface tension is negative throughout the process at lower laser power (2100 W or 2400 W), resulting in an outward flow pattern in the melt pool. However, the flow transitions to an inward flow pattern with increasing laser power due to the excursion crossing the flow transition line [32]. The surface flow carries the high-temperature liquid metal from the top to the bottom of the melt pool, leading to the deep-penetration region in the center of the melt pool [30]. Interestingly, the microhardness of the coating increases remarkably from 421.68 HV to 506.52 HV when the powder feeding rate is greater than 9 g/min. Moreover, the dilution drops significantly when the powder feeding rate exceeds 9 g/min (Fig. 5 (b)), which in fact is responsible for the microhardness improvement of the coating. A lower dilution means the RHEA coating contains more Mo and Nb elements, which have a significant strengthening effect on RHEAs and lead to an increase in the microhardness, as reported [36–38]. Therefore, the in­ crease of strengthening elements content caused by the decrease in dilution rather than the changes in grain size is the main factor for the improvement of the microhardness of the coating. Fig. 3(c1) - (c4) display the morphological evolution of the coatings obtained by various scanning speeds ranging from 3 mm/s − 9 mm/s. The laser power and the powder feeding rate are set to 2700 W and 9 g/ min, respectively. It is worth noting that the variation in scanning speed resulted in a different regularity from most reports in this experiment [39–41]. Specifically, the deep-penetration phenomenon occurs at higher scanning speeds (7 mm/s, 9 mm/s), which means that more energy is absorbed by the substrate in this condition. This phenomenon also occurs at high laser power or low powder feeding rate (Fig. 3(c3) and (c4)). Moreover, the depth and width of the coating decrease first and then increase, as shown in Fig. 6(a) and (b). This can be attributed to the complex impact of scanning speed on the melt pool. On the one hand, the increase in scanning speed could decrease the melt pool temperature and reduce the melt depth due to the reduced laser specific energy E (Eq. (4)) [42], corresponding to the increase in scanning speed from 3 mm/s to 5 mm/s in this experiment. On the other hand, the in­ crease in scanning speed may also raise the melt pool temperature and deepen the melt pool, because the reduction of the powder mass per unit track length (PF/SS) weakens the attenuation effect of the powder stream on the laser energy. So, it could be inferred that the effect of the reduction of laser specific energy (E) dominates until the scanning speed exceeds 7 mm/s. Mathematical models and ANOVA The variance analysis (ANOVA) was used to validate the reason­ ability of the chosen model, which was performed using a stepwise model and eliminating insignificant impact factors. The detailed anal­ ysis procedure for dilution and the corresponding validation parameters are presented in Table 4. Several fundamental values should be noted. First, the p-value for the dilution model is 0.0002, less than 0.005, which means that the possi­ bility of a large F-value of the model caused by noise is only 0.02 %. Meanwhile, the insignificance of “Lack of Fit” also explains that the model is accurate (P greater than 0.005). A larger F-value suggests that the corresponding factor has a more significant influence on the dilution model. In this case, laser power (LP), scanning speed (SS), and powder feeding rate (PF) exhibit a similar level of influence (significant) on the dilution of the coating. In addition, the closer the R2 value is to 1, the better the model fit is [43]. The R2 value of up to 0.8962 means that the experimental data are pretty close to the predictive data. The difference between Adjusted R2 and Predicted R2 is less than the required value of 0.2, indicating a good coincidence [44]. Finally, the adequate precision value, i.e., the ratio of effective signal to noise, is greater than 0.4, manifesting that the model has sufficiently accurate signals [23]. In summary, this model can be used to predict the dilution of the MoNbTiZr RHEA coating in the design space. It can be observed that the ANOVA of porosity meets the above re­ quirements of the fundamental values, as shown in Table 5. The porosity model demonstrates a higher fitting accuracy (0.9323), suggesting that the range of parameters chosen for this experiment is reasonable. Notably, the F-values of SS and PF are substantially larger than those of LP, which signifies that SS and PF have a stronger influence on porosity. Table 6 summarizes the ANOVA results for the microhardness of the RHEA coating. A sufficiently sizeable R2 value proves the accuracy of the model. Besides, SS also exhibits a remarkably higher F-value than LP and PF. In conclusion, it is surprising that SS is the most important pro­ cessing parameter in laser processing of the MoNbTiZr RHEA coating.
