However, porosity defects is becoming a challenge for laser mirror welding due to dual laser beams and trans­ verse welding direction. In this study, experimental analysis and numerical simulation modeling are used to reveal the characteristics and evolution mechanism of porosity in laser mirror welding of 2219 aluminum alloy. Compared with metallurgical porosity related to hydrogen, the size of process porosity is larger, the shape is more irregular, and the inner wall is rougher. The fluctuation of the coupled keyhole is the root cause of the formation of process porosity, manifesting as high-speed fluids on the wall. It is investigated that large process porosities mainly occur near the upper fusion line, which can be divided into two types: α region in the center and β region near the weld surface. The bubbles, which are formed owing to the collapse of the central keyhole in α region and the detachment of keyhole hump in β region individually, follow with upward melt flow by buoyancy. They are captured before crystallization resulting in the concentrated distribution of porosities in these regions. 1. Laser welding has developed rapidly in recent years with the advantages of high energy density, deep penetration, high welding efficiency, and high automaticity [5]. It has become one of the promising options for the connection of aluminum alloy, especially in spacecraft, aircraft and other fields that need to reduce weight [6]. Nevertheless, single laser welding for medium thickness plate of 2219 aluminum alloy has a weakness in lack of penetration and large angular distortion, which results in the decline of structural performance and assembly discomfort. On the basis of inheriting the advantages of single laser beam, laser mirror welding inputs the symmetrical energy on both sides of the butt joint structure, which can solve the above problems effectively [7]. However, porosity is a typical defect in laser welding of aluminum alloy [8], which also exists in laser mirror welding of 2219 aluminum alloy. Experimental details The 2219 aluminum alloy, after aging treatment, is used as the base metal of laser mirror welding, the chemical composition is listed in Table 1. The dimension of 100 × 50 × 6 mm3 is designed for the aluminum alloy plates. Before welding, the plates are polished, cleaned and dried to remove the oil and oxide layer on the surface. The process of laser mirror welding for 2219 aluminum alloy is shown in Fig. 1. The welding system consists of a disk laser (12003, TruDisk), a splitter, two 6-axis robots (KR30HA, KUKA) and two laser welding heads. Due to the horizontal welding position, two aluminum alloy plates are held on the welding platform by a special fixture in Fig. 1 (a) and (d). Two laser beams separated from the splitter are controlled by two robots respectively, creating a symmetrical pattern perpendic­ ular to the weld. Two laser beams move simultaneously to form a penetrating pool and a keyhole, which are protected by 99.99% argon gas at a flow rate of 15 L/min, as exhibited in Fig. 1(b-c). The laser power is 3000 W, the welding speed is at the range of 2–2.7 m/min. Two types of porosity in laser welding of aluminum alloy are investigated: metallurgical porosity and process porosity. It is well confirmed that the formation of metallurgy porosity is related to hydrogen, the solubility of which decreases rapidly in the aluminum alloy as it changes from liquid to solid [28,29]. Fig. 7 shows morphology and element content of a representative metallurgical porosity in α re­ gion of weld seam, which is approximately 49.5 μm in diameter. Columnar and equiaxed dendrites around the porosity are irregularly arranged due to the competition growth of grains. Fig. 7(b) reveals that the porosity is regular spherical with smooth inner sidewalls and partially laminar precipitation. According to the elements on porosity wall, the Cu element at point A on the smooth surface is less than that in the base metal. And it is observed that Oxygen is adsorbed to the porosity wall. As for point B on the layer, the contents of Cu element and O element are increased. It is noteworthy that the high Cu content may be related to the precipitation of the second phase particles. Fig. 8 shows a smaller metallurgical porosity in β region, which is regular spherical with prominent grain morphology on the inner wall. The results of EDS illustrate that compared with the grain boundary, the content of Cu element inside the grain increases, but the content of O elements decreases. This is caused by the element migration and the non-equilibrium crystallization during the laser mirror welding. The white particles appear on the grain with high content of Cu element, which are speculated to be the second phase particle η2 (AlCu) and θ (Al2Cu). The two types of porosities are observed in Fig. 9(a), both of which inhibit the growth of dendrites. The size of process porosity is about 157.2 μm, which is much greater than metallurgical porosity of 25.5 μm. Meanwhile, compared with metallurgical porosity, the shape of process porosity is more irregular, and the inner wall is much rougher. Fig. 9(b) shows the morphology of another process porosity, the wall of which inherits disordered protrusions from the unstable keyhole and disorga­ nized melt flow. The content of Cu element is decreased, but the content of Fe and O element is increased in comparison to the base metal. Since the formation of metallurgical porosities is widely recognized, most of the pores are formed during solidification and exist in the molten pool for a short time. The distribution of metallurgical pores is not biased, therefore, the formation of process pores in two typical areas is the focus of this paper. The reasons for the distribution of process porosities will be illustrated in the later section. 4.2. Porosity evolution mechanism Based on the keyhole behavior and molten pool flow characteristics, the evolution mechanisms of porosities in two areas of the weld seam are analyzed respectively. The porosities in α region are usually formed by the collapse of keyhole, which undergoes fluctuation, necking and in­ termediate collapse, as indicated in Fig. 14(c). Although a through keyhole is formed by two keyholes, multiple reflections inside two laser beams destabilize the keyhole. The instability of the large keyhole makes the middle weaker, which can be seen from the presence of high velocity in Fig. 12(a). Usually, the tip of keyhole in single laser welding is easiest to close [30]. For laser mirror welding, the junction of the two keyhole tips, i.e., the middle of coupled keyhole, is the easiest to collapse due to the instability. The pressure equilibrium at the keyhole wall is what keeps the stability of keyhole [31], bumps and depressions occur on the wall when the balance is disturbed. The recoil pressure (Pr) and the vapor pressure (Pv) tend to maintain the keyhole open, while the surface tension (Ps) and hydrostatic pressures (Pm) are inclined to make it close. 9 X. Yin et al.
